# N1098L Spy Plane

A US Army-contracted Bombardier Global 6500 operated by **LASAI Aviation II LLC** (a Leidos-controlled defense contractor) made low-altitude, low-speed passes near the UVU campus on September 10, 2025. The aircraft is part of the **HADES program** (High Accuracy Detection & Exploitation System) and is publicly described as ISR-capable, including drone deployment and recovery.

## Aircraft identification

* **Tail number:** N1098L (formerly registered as N911PV)
* **Type:** Bombardier Global 6500 (G6500)
* **ICAO:** A0299E
* **Callsign on Sept 10, 2025:** AXLE10
* **Operator:** LASAI Aviation II LLC (control fell under Leidos in 2021)
* **Reported contractor associations:** Beyond LASAI Aviation II LLC / Leidos, citizen investigators have also tied the aircraft to **Sierra Nevada Corp** and **SAIC** within the broader HADES training/ISR contracting context.
* **Program affiliation:** US Army HADES — Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance
* **Fleet sister ship:** LASAI Aviation operates other similar Global 6500s supporting Army reconnaissance, such as [N2100L](/Planes/N2100L) — indicating N1098L was one airframe in a fleet, not a one-off charter.

## Flight pattern on September 10, 2025

According to publicly available ADS-B data via Flightradar24 and ADS-B Exchange:

* Departed **Biggs Army Airfield (KBIF)**, El Paso, TX at approximately 07:48 MDT.
* Arrived **Glacier Park International Airport (KGPI)**, Kalispell, MT at approximately 10:38 MDT.
* Made a **600 ft AGL pass** over Orem/Utah Lake at approximately 11:47 AM MDT (~3 hours before the shooting).
* Made a **335 ft AGL pass** at approximately 12:24 PM MDT, within roughly two miles of the UVU campus — close behind the 12:23 PM MDT shooting.
* Citizen investigators report that the passes ran as low as **~204 ft AGL** and at slow speeds of roughly **118 knots**, an unusually low-and-slow profile directly adjacent to or over the campus.
* Returned through the same corridor approximately 20–24 minutes after the shooting (some accounts describe the return pass as roughly 25 minutes later).

## Public framing of the passes

Researchers have offered competing explanations:

* The official-sounding rationale circulating in defense-aviation forums describes the maneuvers as **drone retrieval training**, with the 600 ft and 335 ft passes characterized as textbook drop/recovery simulations — i.e., routine training or defense operations.
* Independent investigators including Candace Owens and Ian Carroll point to the geometry, timing, and ISR capability of the aircraft as warranting a federal investigation rather than a training-exercise dismissal.
* Citizen investigator John Cullen has, according to his public commentary, described N1098L as a CIA-linked or military ISR asset capable of drone launch and retrieval or signals intelligence (SIGINT) collection, and has theorized it could have been conducting real-time surveillance of the event or otherwise involved in the operation. These remain unproven theories; there is no official confirmation of any direct involvement.

Extensive online discussion has flagged the aircraft as highly suspicious due to the combination of timing, low altitude, and ISR capabilities. Investigators have also scrutinized the aircraft's **later flights**, including a subsequent leg toward the Washington, D.C. area.

No public statement from LASAI, Leidos, or the US Army has named the crew, mission authorization, or operational tasking for the September 10 sortie.

## Open questions

1. Who authorized N1098L's low-altitude passes near a major political event?
2. Were drones in fact deployed or recovered during the passes?
3. Who was on the aircraft, and were any of them in contact with US or foreign intelligence personnel?
4. Why was the aircraft positioned to overfly the kill zone at 335 ft AGL?
5. What was the aircraft's tasking on the return leg 25 minutes after the assassination?

## Track This Aircraft

Research this aircraft's flight history yourself on both major flight-tracking sites. They differ in one important way:

* [FlightRadar24 — N1098L](https://www.flightradar24.com/data/aircraft/n1098l) — large commercial tracker that honors the FAA's [LADD](https://www.faa.gov/pilots/ladd) block list, so flights an owner or the government has asked to hide can be missing.
* [ADS-B Exchange — N1098L](https://globe.adsbexchange.com/?reg=N1098L) — volunteer-fed, unfiltered tracker that does **not** honor LADD, so it often shows blocked aircraft and tracks the filtered sites leave out.
