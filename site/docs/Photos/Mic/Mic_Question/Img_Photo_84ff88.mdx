## What This Image Shows

A concept graphic headed IEM DETONATION in bold white type on a near-black background. The central figure is a semi-translucent glowing cyan render of a human skull, jaw, cervical vertebrae, collarbones and upper ribs. Beside the skull temple and ear, an in-ear monitor is illustrated mid-explosion: a clear plastic housing revealing gold circuitry, micro-wiring and metallic drivers, with yellow and orange sparks, dark debris and smoke bursting from its rear, outlined in red. A thick red arrow runs from the exploding earpiece down toward the neck of the skeleton, and fragments are shown travelling into the neck and collarbone region. A small photographic inset in the lower right shows a man squinting, with red overlays marking his ear and neck.

This is a third variant of the concealed-device argument. Where one strand places a charge in the lapel transmitter and another in a crucifix pendant, this one proposes an in-ear monitor — the earpiece a speaker would wear to hear program audio. The attraction of the variant, for its proponents, is that it puts the device close to the ear and temple, which is where at least one researcher has argued blood first appears.

It is a concept illustration, not proof. No earpiece, charge, or fragment has been recovered or identified in this case, and the official finding is a rifle gunshot. The competing device-placement variants are documented on [Microphone — The Exploding Mic Theory](/Mic/overview), [The Exploding Pendant](/Mic/exploding-pendant-shaped-charge), and [Mic vs Pendant](/Mic/Mic_vs_Pendant).
