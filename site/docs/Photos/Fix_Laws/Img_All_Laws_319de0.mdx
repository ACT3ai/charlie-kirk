## What This Image Shows

A digital composite built as the banner for the four proposed federal disclosure laws. [**Charlie Kirk**](/People/charlie-kirk) is on the left, shown from the chest up holding a black dynamic microphone, standing in front of an outdoor campus building. [**Candace Owens**](/People/candace-owens) is on the right, holding a magnifying glass in a dim office stacked with documents. A soft diagonal shadow divides the two halves. Red banner strips carry the overlay text, and three stylized labels sit across the top center pointing back into the backgrounds: [**NSA**](/US_Intelligence/nsa), [**CIA**](/US_Intelligence/cia), and [**FBI**](/FBI/overview).

The composition states the argument without a sentence of explanation. On one side, the man killed at [Utah Valley University](/UVU/overview) on **September 10, 2025**, at the moment he did the thing he was known for — standing at a campus table with a microphone, taking questions. On the other, the investigator who has spent the time since going through paper. Between them, the three agencies that hold records the public has not been shown. That is the premise of the reform section: the answer is in files that already exist.

The four laws the graphic fronts are modeled on the Epstein Files Transparency Act — **FBI and DOJ Disclosure**, **Intelligence Disclosure**, **Mandate the Investigation**, and **Trusted Investigators**. They are drafted to compel production of records within fixed deadlines rather than to accuse anyone. Nothing in this graphic, and nothing in the laws behind it, asserts that any agency or any named official committed a crime; the case made is that disclosure should be a legal obligation and not a favor. Read the full set at [New Laws (Fix)](/Fix/overview) and the launch article at [Four Laws to Force the Truth](/laws/explain/all/overview).
