## What This Image Shows

A Google Trends screenshot. The search term is **Russell Kim Kennington**, the window is the past 90 days, and the chart shows a single sharp spike reaching a value of 100 on **July 31, 2025**. Two hand-drawn purple ellipses circle the filter menus, and those filters are the reason the screenshot exists: the region is set to **Israel** and the search type to **Image Search**.

The researcher's argument is a timing argument. **July 31, 2025** is roughly six weeks before **September 10, 2025**. A spike in image searches for a specific individual's name, from a specific country, before the event, is the pattern researchers have flagged repeatedly in this investigation — the same method applied to judges, locations, and other names.

The limits have to be stated plainly. Google Trends reports relative interest, not absolute volume; on a name with almost no baseline traffic, a handful of queries can register as 100. A spike here does not identify who searched, or why, and it is not evidence that anyone did anything. It is a question worth answering.
