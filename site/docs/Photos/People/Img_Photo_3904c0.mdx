## What This Image Shows

A two-by-two collage of mobile Google Trends screenshots, separated by thick orange borders and watermarked **[@BasedSamParker](/Influencers/x/based-sam-parker) [@SamParkerSenate](/Influencers/x/samparkersenate)**. All four query the same search term, "skyler Baird," over the same window, 3/1/25 to 9/9/25 — that is, ending the day before the shooting.

The four regional filters produce four different results. **Tel Aviv District** shows a peak of 100 on May 24, 2025. **District of Columbia** shows 72 on March 3, 2025 and a peak of 100 on July 31, 2025. **Haifa District** shows a peak of 100 on July 3, 2025. **Utah** returns nothing at all: "Hmm, your search doesn't have enough data to show here."

The implied argument is that a name was being searched in [Israel](/Israel/overview) and Washington before it meant anything in Utah. That argument has a serious methodological problem the poster does not address: Google Trends normalises to 100 within each region, so a peak of 100 in a low-volume region can represent a handful of searches, and the tool routinely returns noise for uncommon names. This is presented as a claim by the account that made it, not as a finding.
