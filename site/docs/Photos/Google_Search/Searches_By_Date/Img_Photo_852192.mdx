## What This Image Shows

Three **Google Trends** panels stacked in one capture, all filtered to the **District of Columbia**, **Past 90 days**. The terms and readings are "**mauser 98 velocity**" on **August 6, 2025** at **100**, "**hades n1098l**" on **August 5, 2025** at **84**, and "**Justin Streiff**" on **August 2, 2025** at **100**.

This is the panel researchers rate highest in the date-stacked set, and the reason is the specificity of two of the three strings. "mauser 98 velocity" is a ballistics query about a particular antique rifle action — the weapon type later recovered in this case — rather than a general firearms search. "hades n1098l" pairs the name of a U.S. Army airborne sensor program with the exact tail number of the aircraft that flew low over Utah Valley on [September 10, 2025](/Timeline/Sept_10_2025). Neither string has meaningful organic search demand. Both peak in the first week of **August 2025**, five weeks before the event.

The limits still hold and should be stated plainly: Trends gives a normalized index inside one metro bucket, so a peak of 100 on a near-zero baseline can rest on a handful of queries, and the tool discloses nothing about who searched or why. No person is accused of anything here.
