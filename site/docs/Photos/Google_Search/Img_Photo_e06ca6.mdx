## What This Image Shows

A four-by-four collage of sixteen separate **Google Trends** panels. Every panel shows the same signature — a flat baseline broken by one spike to an index of 100 — and the tooltips step forward roughly a week at a time from early **May 2025** through late **July 2025**. The region filters are **Israel**, **Tel Aviv District**, or **Center District**, with ranges of Past 12 months, Past 90 days, or a custom window ending **9/10/25**.

The sixteen terms, in order, are: "utah valley university roof" (May 4-10), "lance twiggs discount" (May 4-10), "uvu layout" (May 9-15), "Phil lyman" (May 11-17), "Eric Bolling" (May 18-24), "Tyler Robinson caldera" (May 25-31), a further "lance twiggs" query (Jun 1-7), "Professor mark harlin" (Jun 8-14), "lance twiggs lds" (Jun 22-28), "Dr. Elena Vasquez" (Jun 29 - Jul 5), "tyler robinson discount" (Jul 6-12), "Tempanogos Regional Hospital" (Jul 6-12), "Rusty needs" (Jul 17), "Tyler Lance twiggs" (Jul 18), "Phil lyman" again (Jul 18), and "phil lyman Tiffany Barber" (Jul 20-26).

The grid format is the argument: the claim is not about any one panel but about a near-continuous chain of one-week peaks marching through the summer across campus layout queries, the man later charged and his roommate, a Utah political figure, a hospital name, and names tied to the disputed flight crew. The rebuttal is equally structural — a grid built by selecting sixteen terms that each happen to peak in a different week can be assembled from any large pool of low-volume queries, because a low-volume term always peaks somewhere. Nothing here identifies a searcher or shows that anyone knew anything in advance.
