## About This Cluster

This cluster holds the **Google Trends** and Google search-result screenshots that citizen researchers captured after [**September 10, 2025**](/Timeline/Sept_10_2025). The premise behind the whole thread is simple: Google Trends publishes the geography and timing of search interest in any term, and researchers went looking to see whether the names, addresses, aircraft, hospitals, and officials that only became relevant *after* the shooting at [**Utah Valley University**](/UVU/overview) were already being searched *before* it. The screenshots posted to X claim to show exactly that — spikes attributed to **Israel** and to the **Washington D.C.** metro bucket in the weeks and months ahead of the event, with many clustering around **July 20, 2025**. Trends data cannot identify who ran a search or why they ran it, so everything filed here is a lead, not a finding.

The recurring pattern the posts point to is foreign or federal-corridor search interest appearing where local Utah interest is absent — searches for the spy plane [**N1098L**](/Planes/N1098L/overview), for [**Timpanogos Regional Hospital**](/Medical/Timpanogos_Facility) and its emergency surgeons, for [**Lance Twiggs**](/People/lance-twiggs)'s St. George address, for defense attorney [**Kathryn Nester**](/People/kathryn-nester) in December 2024, and for the judges later assigned to the case. A second thread inside this cluster is the deletion claim: **Stew Peters** said on his show on **July 16, 2026** that after he named specific figures in the [**Fort Huachuca**](/Planes/Airport-Fort-Huachuca) image-search data, "every person we mentioned had their results completely wiped" while unnamed people still returned results. That is his account of what he saw; no record has been published showing that Google, any Google employee, or anyone else removed anything, and Trends and image results routinely change as the tool's trailing window moves. Both threads share the same weakness and the same value — the screenshots are reproducible only if someone archived them, which is why researchers keep capturing and republishing them. Nothing here establishes that any person or agency searched anything; these are the posters' readings of aggregate, anonymized data. And nothing here is an accusation against the people whose names were searched: appearing in a search-interest chart, or being discussed on a broadcast, says nothing whatever about a person's conduct.

Three sub-clusters break the material down. **[Searches By Date](/Photos/Google_Search/Searches_By_Date/overview)** groups the captures by the day the spike lands on, which is where the July 20 anchor argument lives. **[N1098L NCSS CIA](/Photos/Google_Search/Google_N1098L_NCSS_CIA/overview)** collects the aircraft and contractor searches. **[Google Quotes](/Photos/Google_Search/Google_Quotes/overview)** holds the searches run against specific names — including the claimed flight crew — to test whether those people exist in public records at all. For the written analysis behind these images, start at [Google Searches](/GoogleSearches/overview) and its [Investigation Index](/GoogleSearches/investigation-index), then read the [July 20, 2025 anchor](/GoogleSearches/july-20-2025-anchor) and the [deletion and suppression claims](/GoogleSearches/trends-deletion-suppression).
