## What This Image Shows

A broadcast clip shared on X by [@Cashloren](https://x.com/Cashloren/status/1965869689755812061) on **September 10, 2025**, showing **Glenn Beck** on **Megyn Kelly**'s program in the hours after the shooting. Kelly asks whether he is hearing it "from people who are in a position to know that Charlie is alive and that he's in critical" condition. Beck answers: "I am. I spoke to somebody in a position who knows who said **he has a pulse**. So that's good news."

This clip is filed in the wound-location cluster because it documents the same phenomenon from a different angle. In the first hour, confident statements were circulating on live television from people with apparently good sources, and those statements did not hold. Beck was visibly emotional and was relaying what he had been told; nothing here suggests he said anything he did not believe, and no criticism of him or of Kelly is intended or implied.

What researchers take from it is a caution that cuts against their own thread as much as for it. If a well-sourced commentator can relay information on air that turns out to be wrong within the hour, then the uniformity of the early "heart" descriptions is at least as easily explained by the ordinary chaos of live coverage as by anything else.
