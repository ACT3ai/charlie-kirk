## What This Image Shows

A single **Google Trends** panel comparing **Tony graf** and **Robert lunnen** as web-search terms, region set to **Israel**, date range **5/1/25 to 10/19/25**, all categories. A purple watermark carrying **[@BasedSamParker](/Influencers/x/based-sam-parker) [@SamParkerSenate](/Influencers/x/samparkersenate)** sits across the middle. The chart is essentially flat for both terms except for one sharp isolated spike for each.

Isolating the Israel filter is the point of this version. Both names — the retiring judge and his successor — show a single burst of interest from that region inside the same six-month window, and nothing else across the whole span.

The caution is the same as on the dual-panel chart and it is not a small one. Relative-interest data on very low-volume terms is noisy enough that a handful of queries produces a full-height spike, and the window overlaps the period when the appointment was announced and reported. Nothing here identifies who searched or why, and nothing here shows that either judge did anything. It is a question about a pattern, filed so readers can weigh it themselves.
