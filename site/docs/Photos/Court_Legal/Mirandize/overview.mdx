## About This Cluster

Everything in this cluster turns on one question with a very large answer: what time was **Tyler Robinson** actually taken into custody on **September 11, 2025**? A defense filing quotes a discovery video, Bates-stamped **003996-R2**, in which an officer says at internal timestamp **03:36:53**, logged as **06:25 PM**, "So Tyler, um, before we begin, I'm just gonna read you your rights," and one minute later Robinson answers that he would like to speak with counsel, "hoping for a fellow in Utah named Doug Terry." The Discord messages the state treats as a confession are timestamped around **7:57 PM** the same evening. If the 6:25 PM reading happened, and Washington County protocol took his phone at that point, then those messages were typed roughly ninety minutes after he no longer had the phone. Robinson is charged, not convicted, and the seizure time, the account authentication, and the unedited video are all still contested.

The images assemble both sides of that argument from public records. The **Utah County Sheriff inmate booking sheet** lists an arrest date of **09/11/2025 at 10:00 PM** with a Date In of **09/12/2025 at 01:58 AM** and charges under **76-5-202** aggravated murder, **76-8-306** obstruction of justice, and **76-11-210** felony discharge of a firearm. A **Washington County Attorney's Office** Facebook post from **September 12, 2025** is screenshotted with its hover timestamp exposed. Filing pages show the invocation of the Fifth and Sixth Amendment rights argued against public statements officials made afterward. Two AI-generated summaries — one Google **AI Overview**, one Grok explainer — are included precisely because they show the confusion: one says the encounter occurred **September 11**, another says **September 12**, and the arrest is recorded at **4:00 AM on September 12**. Attorney **Baron Coleman** built the early-arrest argument on exactly that spread, noting that arresting officer **Brian Davis** would have had to leave northern Utah around 6:30 PM to reach Washington County by 10 PM. A screenshot of Utah's statutory definition of "arrest" is filed here for the same reason. The counterargument is also documented: a person can lawfully be held on an investigative hold for up to 24 hours before rights are read.
