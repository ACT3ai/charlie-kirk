## What This Image Shows

A high-resolution close-up of a printed filing page. The text transcribes a recorded interview in which **Tyler Robinson** is read his rights and then invokes his right to counsel and his right to remain silent. Key phrases, timestamps and statements are marked with a translucent light-blue highlighter added by whoever shared the page.

The file this image was saved under records the point being made: a Miranda reading at **6:25 PM on September 11, 2025**. That timestamp is the pivot of the whole custody argument. The same encounter is quoted at greater length in defense filings as discovery exhibit **Bates 003996-R2**, where the internal video timestamp **03:36:53** corresponds to 06:25 PM and the request for counsel follows one minute later.

The highlighting is a researcher's, and this crop shows a portion of a longer filing rather than the whole document. What it establishes on its face is that a rights reading and an immediate invocation of counsel are recorded in the court record. What follows from that — whether the messages posted later that evening could have come from the same person — is the argument, not the document.
