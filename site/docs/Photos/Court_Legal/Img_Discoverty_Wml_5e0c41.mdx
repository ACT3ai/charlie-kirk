## What This Image Shows

A screenshot of a computer file directory titled **[Tyler Robinson](/People/tyler-robinson) Court Documents**, showing a grid of 98 PDF icons filed chronologically by date-prefixed filenames. A faint semi-transparent watermark reading **BLAKE BENNETT** lies across the background.

The filenames are the content. They begin **9_16_25** with the acknowledgement, bail order, Information, probable cause affidavit, protective order and public-safety attachment, then run forward: the **9_17_25** initial appearance, a **9_22_25** protective order on publicity, **9_24_25** transport and standing decorum orders, a run of **9_28_25** supplemental notices, **9_30_25** and **10_6_25** discovery requests, a **10_8_25** motion to preserve evidence, **10_13_25** and **10_14_25** stipulated motions and interim orders, and **10_20_25** motions and orders on classifying material as private.

What this image documents is the state of public access. The docket in a capital case arising from a political assassination is not published as a searchable public archive; it is a grid of PDFs one person downloaded, renamed and kept. Everything else in this cluster came out of a folder like this. That dependence on volunteer archiving is one of the specific gaps the reform section proposes to close.
