## What This Image Shows

This **Google Trends** screenshot charts the search term **Sarah Klein** over a 90-day window. Interest is near zero for almost the whole period, punctuated by a single massive vertical spike on **September 4, 2025**, reaching the maximum value of 100. That date is six days before the assassination.

The name is one of seven the research deck says belonged to profiles connected with UVU's Center for National Security Studies, and which it claims were removed from public web pages in late September 2025, after the **September 10, 2025** assassination of **Charlie Kirk** at Utah Valley University. Sarah Klein appears as item E in the deck's lettered list of seven.

Google Trends normalizes every chart to a 0-100 scale, so a spike built from a handful of searches is drawn exactly the same as one built from thousands. The tool reports no searcher identity, no IP address, and no reason for a search. Common names collect unrelated traffic from unrelated people. A country or metro filter tells you where queries originated, not who made them or what they were looking for.

Sarah Klein is a common name shared by many people, including public figures with no connection to Utah or to this case. A normalized spike on a common name is especially weak evidence, because the chart cannot separate one person's traffic from another's.

No person named in this cluster has been charged with or accused of any offense, and no agency has confirmed a link between any of these names and any intelligence service. The chart is one researcher's exhibit, not a finding.
