## What This Image Shows

This **Google Trends** screenshot shows search analytics for **[Elena Vasquez](/People/elena-vasquez)** in **[Israel](/Israel/overview)** over a 90-day period. The interest-over-time graph carries a single sharp spike against an otherwise flat zero baseline.

The name is one of seven the research deck says belonged to profiles connected with UVU's Center for National Security Studies, and which it claims were removed from public web pages in late September 2025, after the **[September 10, 2025](/Timeline/Sept_10_2025)** assassination of **[Charlie Kirk](/People/charlie-kirk)** at [Utah Valley University](/UVU/overview). The 90-day window is the tighter of the two ranges the researcher used, and it is the one that would show any activity close to September 2025.

Google Trends normalizes every chart to a 0-100 scale, so a spike built from a handful of searches is drawn exactly the same as one built from thousands. The tool reports no searcher identity, no IP address, and no reason for a search. Common names collect unrelated traffic from unrelated people. A country or metro filter tells you where queries originated, not who made them or what they were looking for.

No person named in this cluster has been charged with or accused of any offense, and no agency has confirmed a link between any of these names and any intelligence service. The chart is one researcher's exhibit, not a finding.
