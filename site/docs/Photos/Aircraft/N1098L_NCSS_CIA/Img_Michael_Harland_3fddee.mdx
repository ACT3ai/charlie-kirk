## What This Image Shows

A **Google Trends** capture. Search term **Michael Harlan**, region **Washington DC (Hagerstown)**, window **Past 12 months**. The line sits flat at zero for almost the whole period, punctuated by two narrow spikes reaching an index of 100, with no tooltip label showing.

The shape itself is the thing to understand. A flat line at zero broken by two isolated bars is what Google Trends produces when a term has **very low absolute search volume** -- the index is relative, so the busiest single week becomes 100 no matter how few searches that represents. A term nobody searches will still show a spike on the day two people do.

That is why this whole set of charts cannot carry the weight placed on it. Without absolute volumes, which Trends does not publish, a spike is uninterpretable. It is not evidence that anybody in any particular place looked up anybody in particular.
