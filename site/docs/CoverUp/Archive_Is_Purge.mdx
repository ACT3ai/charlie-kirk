# Archive.today

:::caution Legal Disclaimer
Nothing on this page is a claim of fact that any person, agency, or organization knew of, planned, participated in, or covered up any crime, or acted illegally, immorally, or unethically. This page lays out documented events, dates, and attributed public claims — not findings of fact. Everyone named or referenced is presumed to have acted lawfully. [Tyler Robinson](/People/tyler-robinson) is **charged, not convicted**.
:::

**Archive.today** — also reachable as **archive.is**, **archive.ph**, **archive.md**, **archive.fo**, **archive.li**, **archive.vn**, and a Tor address — is a privately run, on-demand web archiving service that has operated since **2012**. A user pastes a URL; the service stores a rendered screenshot plus a text/HTML copy of the page at that moment. It is the main practical alternative to the Internet Archive's **Wayback Machine**, and it is widely used to preserve pages behind paywalls.

The reason it matters to this investigation is narrow and specific: **it is the archive that is hard to delete from.** Archive.today is user-requested rather than broadly crawled, it does not honor robots.txt the way the Wayback Machine does, its servers sit in Europe, and its operator has for years refused most removal requests on the stated grounds that resisting link rot and erasure is the entire point of the service. Citizen investigators who watched source material disappear after **September 10, 2025** treated it as the last durable copy of the record.

On **October 30, 2025**, the FBI issued a subpoena aimed at unmasking the person or entity behind it.

## The date that drew attention: October 30, 2025

The subpoena date is the reason this page exists. Placed on a calendar next to the two other events this investigation tracks, it lands like this:

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **September 10, 2025** | [Charlie Kirk](/People/charlie-kirk) is killed at Utah Valley University. |
| **October 10, 2025** | Building 602 at the **[Accurate Energetic Systems](./AES_Tennessee_Explosion)** explosives plant in Humphreys County, Tennessee detonates, killing 16 workers. |
| **October 13, 2025** | The most-cited post alleging that [witness phone video was deleted or altered remotely](./Videos_Deleted_Remotely) is published on X. |
| **October 30, 2025** | The FBI issues a subpoena to registrar **Tucows** demanding subscriber data on the operator of Archive.today. |
| **November 29, 2025** | The subpoena's stated compliance deadline. |

**Twenty days** separate the AES plant explosion from the subpoena. **Fifty days** separate the killing at UVU from the subpoena. That proximity — and nothing more — is what put this document in front of people investigating the Kirk case.

To be exact about what that does and does not establish: **no public document connects the Archive.today subpoena to Charlie Kirk, to Utah Valley University, or to the Tennessee explosion.** The subpoena does not name a crime at all. The timing is a coincidence unless and until something is produced that shows otherwise, and this page does not assert that anything has been produced. What the date does justify is asking the question, and preserving the record while the question is open.

## What the subpoena actually said

The document itself is public because the operator published it. Per reporting in Ars Technica, 404 Media, The Verge, Gizmodo, Hackread, and heise online:

* It was issued to **Tucows**, the Ontario, Canada-based domain registrar for the archive.today family of domains — not to the archive service directly, and not to the person behind it.
* It was framed as part of **"a federal criminal investigation being conducted by the FBI."**
* It demanded a broad set of identifying records: **customer or subscriber name, addresses, length of service, telephone and text-message records, session times and durations, internet connectivity information, payment and billing details, IP addresses, mobile device identification codes, and the types of services used.**
* It carried a **non-disclosure request** and a compliance deadline of roughly **November 29, 2025**, with the usual warning about court enforcement for non-compliance.
* It **did not specify the alleged crime**. Every outlet that covered it noted this.

This is the ordinary shape of a **criminal investigative subpoena served on a third-party service provider** to identify an anonymous account holder. It is not a lawsuit against Archive.today, not an indictment, not a seizure warrant, and not a takedown order. No charge against the operator has been made public, and no seizure of the site has occurred.

Tucows publicly said it complies with valid legal process while supporting free expression, and declined to comment on an active investigation.

## The "canary"

Archive.today's official X account, **@archiveis**, had been silent for about a year. It broke that silence by posting the subpoena itself with a single word: **"canary."**

That is a deliberate reference to a **warrant canary** — the practice of signaling the existence of a legal demand you may be barred from describing directly. Whatever the non-disclosure request in the document asked for, the effect of the post was that the order became public within days and was covered worldwide.

The operator later commented, through the service's associated blog, that they sit far outside U.S. jurisdiction, that the practical effect of the subpoena on the service was limited, and that the press attention amounted to amplified circulation of the document rather than deep investigative reporting. The operator has never been publicly identified. Registration details reported in the press have long been described as pseudonymous, and independent researchers have speculated for years about the operator's nationality — none of that is confirmed, and it is repeated here only as unverified speculation.

## Why investigators here cared

Two threads on this site converge on the question of whether digital evidence survives.

The first is the allegation, catalogued on **[Witness Videos Deleted Remotely From Phones](./Videos_Deleted_Remotely)**, that several people who filmed the September 10 shooting found their footage **missing, truncated, or altered** by the time they got home, without deleting it themselves. Those are unverified claims — there is no published device-forensics report, no court filing, and no chain-of-custody record confirming any phone was wiped. Separately, this site documents a witness account that an [FBI agent asked for a video to be deleted](./FBI_Asked_Delete_Video) after it was handed over.

The second is the observation that when originals vanish, the archive is the fallback — and that a Wayback snapshot can itself be absent, blocked by a later robots.txt, or never taken in the first place. Archive.today is the copy that is hardest to make disappear.

Put those together and you get the framing that circulated widely in an X post titled **"FBI's DIGITAL PURGE: Erasing Charlie Kirk Assassination Evidence in REAL TIME,"** which asserted that **"Eyewitness vids WIPED, metadata GONE, servers SCRUBBED, links DEAD"** and characterized the subpoena as using a copyright pretext to reach a preservation platform, comparing it to the Megaupload takedown. **Those are that poster's characterizations. This site does not adopt them.** No crime was named in the subpoena, and no copyright theory appears anywhere in it.

Other examples circulated alongside it — reverse image searches returning "scrubbed" results and 404s on old celebrity-photo aggregator pages, a 2023 state-visit video said to be missing from official sites and archives, and Tyler Robinson-linked public profiles said to have been removed after being captured. Each is thinly sourced, and dead links, deleted accounts, expired hosting, platform moderation, and ordinary link rot account for most missing pages most of the time.

## What has happened to the site since

The service **is still online as of mid-2026**, across its domain mirrors, though reachability fluctuates. It was not seized, blocked, or shut down by the 2025 subpoena. It has, however, taken serious damage from a direction unrelated to any of the above:

* **January 11, 2026** — Researchers reported that JavaScript served on Archive.today's CAPTCHA page was directing visitors' browsers to repeatedly hit the blog of an individual critic, functioning as a distributed denial-of-service against that blog. Volunteers also documented archived pages that appeared to have been **edited after capture** to insert that critic's name into the stored content.
* **February 7–20, 2026** — English Wikipedia ran a request for comment on those findings and, on **February 20, 2026**, deprecated Archive.today, added its domains to the spam blacklist, and began removing links — reported at roughly **695,000 citations across some 400,000 pages**.
* **March 2026** — Reporting described Cloudflare categorizing the domains as command-and-control/botnet activity, breaking resolution through Cloudflare's filtered DNS resolvers for some users.

That last cluster cuts in an important direction for anyone using this page: the archive that was valued precisely because it is hard to alter has itself been **credibly accused of altering stored captures**. Both things can be true — that it resists takedowns, and that a single anonymous operator with full write access to the archive is a single point of failure. It is a reason to keep independent copies rather than to trust any one service.

The service has also been blocked or restricted at the national level in several countries, and has been in continuous friction with publishers over paywall circumvention since long before 2025.

## Ordinary explanations, stated plainly

These deserve real weight, and several of them are stronger than the timing argument:

* **Registrar subpoenas are routine.** Federal investigators serve them constantly, for fraud, harassment, threats, intellectual property, and computer crime. Nothing in this one points at Utah.
* **Archive.today has drawn complaints for over a decade.** It is the best-known paywall bypass on the internet and has been an obvious target of publisher and rights-holder pressure since long before September 2025.
* **The document names no crime, so no crime can be inferred from it.** Reading a Kirk connection into a blank field is interpretation, not evidence.
* **Fall 2025 was a busy period for this kind of order.** A subpoena issued in a given month and unrelated events in adjacent months coincide constantly; that is what calendars do.
* **The 2026 findings supply an independent, non-conspiratorial reason for governments and platforms to scrutinize the service** — and they postdate the subpoena, so they cannot have motivated it, but they do show the service was capable of generating its own legal exposure.
* **The FBI is presumed to have acted lawfully.** No court has found that the bureau or any official destroyed, altered, or suppressed evidence in this case.

## What to do about it

The practical takeaway does not depend on resolving any of the above. If a page, video, or document matters to this case, **do not rely on one archive.** Capture it to the Wayback Machine and Archive.today, save a local copy of the file itself with its metadata intact, and record the hash. The reason this page sits under Cover Up (Possible) is not that anything has been proven — it is that the durability of the open-source record is an open question, and the answer is entirely in the hands of whoever bothers to keep copies.
