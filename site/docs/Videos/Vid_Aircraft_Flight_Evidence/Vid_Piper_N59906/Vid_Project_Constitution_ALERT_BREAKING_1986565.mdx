An 8 minute compilation posted by Project Constitution. It is stitched from at
least four sources: a split-screen podcast conversation between a host and a
caller, a vertical commentary insert from a third person, a segment of a
broadcast panel show, and finally raw event footage with graphic annotations
laid over it. It ends on shaky handheld phone video from inside the crowd.

The construction matters, because the second half contains material that argues
against the first half, and the video runs both without reconciling them.

## What Is Said

The caller does most of the talking in the opening half and her subject is drone
footage. She credits two social media accounts with the original release and
thanks people in a group chat for helping verify it overnight. Her verification
claim is specific and testable, which is to her credit: "we checked every single
piece of that drone footage. It corroborated with the parking lot and even
Tyler's FBI footage from the Utah campus. Same cars in the parking lot, same
drone footage in the parking lot. So this isn't AI, it's not fake."

She then describes what she says the drone covered: "it went everywhere. It went
to the underground tunnel. It went to the bushes, where the gun was." The host
interrupts -- "wait, it went to the underground tunnel" -- and she says she has
footage of the aircraft in the air at 12:07 and has slowed it down frame by
frame.

Her stated purpose goes further than the footage: "what we're doing is not really
just showing that the drone is there. We're showing why the drone is there and
who put the drone in the air, allegedly." She points at a still of an older man
photographed entering the venue with the event party and says he is a drone
specialist who has worked in Utah for years, that she found his social media
accounts, and that this is "the reason why he's with them, because most likely he
is a person that was legally able to have a drone in the air."

That passage is an allegation about a private individual built from a photograph
and a social media search, hedged twice by the speaker's own words -- "allegedly"
and "most likely". This site does not identify that person, does not adopt the
inference, and does not suggest that anybody shown in that still did anything
improper. It is recorded here as a claim the video makes.

Her actual complaint is narrower and clearer: "why did they lie and say there was
no drones in the air if there was a drone in the air from their own people." She
pre-empts the obvious rebuttal, that a pre-event drone shoot would be routine, by
arguing on timing: "there have been some people online saying, well, the drone was
there 15 minutes before, they're allowed to do that if they want. Well, no. The
drone was there way after 12 o'clock, way after 12:07 when the students were in
the courtyard." She also asserts that flying over people is not permitted in
Utah.

From about 4:36 a different voice takes over and the video's own argument turns
around. A panel speaker, presented as speaking in defence of the protection
detail, explains jurisdiction: on a college campus a private detail must work

proximity", and they had to "rely on PD to secure the larger perimeter". Another
voice adds the gap plainly: "we don't have counter snipers. We don't have the
Secret Service." He also says many campus police departments have no drone
programme at all and argues that they should, by law.

The last spoken passage is a first-hand recollection from someone who arrived
with the party. He says they were looking at aerial footage of the crowd an hour

There's too many buildings." He describes the response as a shrug.

Then the audio cuts to the plaza -- the tail of the final question, an
exclamation, and shouting to run.

## What The Footage Shows

Three-dimensional satellite flyovers of the campus and its amphitheatre; the
split-screen podcast layout; a still photograph of the event party arriving; a
vertical commentary insert; a studio panel at a desk; then, from about 5:28, real
aerial footage panning over the packed crowd; a card reading "PROOF OF DRONES";
and a long annotated sequence in which red circles, arrows and magnifier callouts
track a small dark multi-rotor aircraft against the sky. The final fifteen
seconds are low-angle handheld phone footage ending on the bang and the crowd
breaking.

Weighing it: the annotated sequence does appear to show a small aircraft aloft
over the venue, and the corroboration method the caller describes -- matching
parked cars between the aerial footage and other released material -- is a real
method and a reasonable one. What is asserted rather than shown is everything
downstream of that: the tunnel and undergrowth coverage, the 12:07 timestamp, the
operator's identity, and the intent. None of those appear on screen in this cut.

The most valuable thirty seconds in the whole eight minutes are the jurisdiction
explanation, because it supplies a mundane structural account of the same rooftop
failure the rest of the video treats as sinister, and the video is honest enough
to include it.
