## What This Video Is

Two minutes twenty-six seconds, cut from Candace Owens' show and reposted by the account MJTruthUltra, whose handle appears in the lower third alongside a tips email address for Owens' site.

Owens is at a desk in a home studio with a laptop and a podcast microphone; a blue sideboard with books and a plant is behind her, a crucifix on the wall, and a large mug on the desk with "CIA" across it. The segment cuts between medium shots of her talking and full-screen graphics of four vehicles.

It is not footage of anything. It is a presenter reading out research she says she was given, with supporting graphics — and, at the end, an appeal to the public for corroboration.

## What Is Said

Owens speaks throughout. She credits the material to a source rather than to her own work — "another mommy sleuth", her term for the volunteer researchers who send her material.

The claim, in her words: "I learned that oddly, Duncan Aviation — you know, where that plane was transponding from, the FBO — in Provo sourced rental cars for these quote unquote Egyptians when they landed a couple of days before this operation."

Two things in that sentence deserve separating. The checkable part is that a fixed-base operator at Provo arranged hire vehicles for arriving passengers, which is a routine service an FBO provides to any arriving aircraft. The unsupported part is the framing — "these quote unquote Egyptians", "this operation" — which asserts, without evidence offered in the segment, that the arrivals were something other than what their aircraft's registration says they were and that an operation took place. This site does not adopt that framing. No person connected with that aircraft has been charged with anything, and nothing in this segment establishes that anyone did anything.

She then says she obtained the registration plates of four hired vehicles and reads all four out on air, with each vehicle shown full-screen as a graphic — a silver 2025 Toyota RAV4, a white 2025 Toyota saloon, a white 2023 Toyota RAV4, and a black 2025 Toyota SUV, with two states of registration between them. **The plate numbers are deliberately not reproduced on this page.** Publishing vehicle registrations attached to people who have been accused of nothing is not something this site does, and the identifications are unverified in any case.

She also frames a political ask around the disclosure — "surely Tulsi's office can get involved" — a reference to the Director of National Intelligence.

The last forty seconds are the operative part, and they are a crowdsourcing appeal aimed at a specific group of people: "To the students at UVU, please check your phones, your pictures from that day, because if any of those cars were seen at UVU or around campus on that day, then we're going to be able to suspect something else. Check hotels, maybe gas stations, maybe you work somewhere… look for those plates during that time frame from when those cars were there until they departed." Tips are directed to an email address on her site.

There is a brief production aside audible mid-segment — Owens asking off-mic whether the graphics were actually shown on screen, and being told they were — which confirms the plates were displayed as well as spoken.

## What The Footage Shows

Static medium and medium-close shots of the presenter, and four full-screen product-style vehicle images on plain backgrounds with a black text box carrying a plate number beneath each one.

Those images are stock or catalogue photographs of vehicle models, not photographs of the specific cars. Nothing on screen shows a vehicle at Provo, at UVU, or anywhere else. There is no rental agreement, no invoice, no FBO record, no timestamped photograph, and no named source. The evidentiary content of the segment is entirely the presenter's spoken assertion of what she was told, plus four plate strings whose provenance is not shown.

That is worth being plain about, because the appeal at the end asks members of the public to go looking for those plates in their own photographs. Any match found that way would be a member of the public matching a string they were given on a podcast to a car in a snapshot, with no chain of custody at either end. It is not nothing — a genuine sighting could be a real lead — but it is the weakest possible form of vehicle identification, and it puts private individuals who happen to drive similar cars at some risk of being wrongly pointed at.
