## What This Video Is

Three minutes and fifty seconds of assembled analysis published by Project Constitution under a headline claiming high-altitude military drones at UVU. It is not raw footage. It is an edit: crowd video with red annotations, ground-level zoom footage, document screenshots, split screens, synchronised feeds with timecode overlays, and a closing card reading "fin." A graphic partway through credits the handle `@HustleBitch_`, suggesting at least some of the source video came from another poster. The archive holds it with post identifier 1970986606845710336. It has not been published to IPFS, so a still frame stands in for the player.

Of the drone material in this collection, this is the piece that actually attempts a demonstration rather than an assertion. That does not make its conclusion right, but it makes it checkable, which is a real difference.

## What The Footage Shows

The reel is structured in five moves.

* **00:00 - 00:13** — A high, wide, static shot of the campus courtyard packed with people. A red circle marks a dark spot in the sky. A second circle marks a person standing on a balcony.
* **00:13 - 00:32** — Text slides and document screenshots on black, setting out the university's drone policy and a records-request response.
* **00:33 - 00:54** — Handheld footage from ground level, low angle, looking up past a balcony into the sky. A red circle tracks the object; the camera zooms until it occupies roughly a tenth of the frame.
* **00:55 - 01:40** — A slow tracking pan holding the object against the mountain backdrop, zooming in and out while keeping it centred.
* **01:41 - 03:49** — The demonstration: a red arrow tracing the object's path over the wide campus shot, a split screen pairing the wide view with a zoomed circular view, then multiple synchronised feeds with timecode overlays following the object across the sky, close stabilised tracking shots, and a return to the annotated wide shot.

What this establishes, and what it does not, should be kept apart.

It establishes reasonably well that a small airborne object was over that crowd and was filmed from more than one position — the synchronised feeds and the tracking pan are the strongest part of the reel, because they are hard to fake casually and they let a viewer follow the same object across independent angles. A TPUSA logo is visible on a tent in the courtyard, fixing the location and occasion.

It does not establish what the object was. At the zoom levels shown, a small quadcopter, a large consumer drone, a bird and a distant aircraft are not reliably distinguishable, and nothing in the reel offers a scale reference, an altitude calculation, or an identification of make or type. The headline word "military" is a claim in the title, not a finding in the footage. The reel's own strongest material is consistent with a drone being present; it is not, by itself, consistent with any particular conclusion about whose it was.

The document material is a different kind of evidence and the more durable kind. If the policy screenshots and the records-request response are what they appear to be, they speak to whether any drone was authorised over that event — a question that has a paper answer somewhere, and that other pages on this site pursue through records rather than through video.
