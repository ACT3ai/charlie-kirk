## What This Video Is

A clip circulated on X in March 2026 by the account "Red Pill Dispenser" (post
`2038934507307343872`), pinned to IPFS by this investigation. It is a
**frame-by-frame walkthrough** of the tent footage from Utah Valley University,
narrated by **Candace Owens** over the frames she is examining.

No transcription or automated visual description has been produced for this file
yet, so this page is written from the clip's established provenance and from the
detailed account of its content already published on the site's
[Tyler Robinson](/People/tyler-robinson) page, where the same video is embedded
as the second of three ballistics clips. It is one of the more consequential
short videos in the archive, because it is the clearest statement of the
ballistics objection by the person who has pushed it hardest into public view.

## What Is Said

The video is Owens talking through footage she says was shown to her. Her
argument is built in four steps.

**One: the round did not pass through.** Her stated observation from the frames
is that the round *"didn't go through - it not only did not go through, it didn't
even cause any sort of a mess."*

**Two: the shirt.** She argues that Kirk's white shirt stayed white until he hit
the ground - that the visible blood arrives later than the impact, not with it.
That is the specific frame-timing claim the site examines separately at
[The Shirt Before the Blood](/Suspicious/Cause_of_Death/shirt-before-blood).

**Three: no backward spray.** She states there is **zero blood travelling
backward** in the frames after the hit. This is the observation that matters most
to the ballistics question, because a high-velocity rifle round is generally
expected to produce material moving away from the shooter.

**Four: the conclusion.** She states it without hedging:

> "That instantly felt wrong to me for what they are telling us took place here,
> which is a 30-aught-six... I will never buy the narrative that a 30-aught-six
> is what killed Charlie Kirk. I'm just not accepting that narrative whatsoever."

She also flags a detail that belongs to a different thesis rather than to the
ballistics one: the motion of the necklace and its magnetic clasp, which feeds
the separate line of enquiry catalogued under
[The Microphone](/Mic/overview).

Everything in this section is **Owens's reading of the frames**, and it is
reported here as her stated view. She is describing what she believes she sees;
she is not presenting a forensic report, and no wound examination, autopsy
finding, or ballistic test is cited in the clip. The site records her argument
because it is a specific, frame-anchored claim that can be tested against the
footage rather than a general suspicion.

## What The Footage Shows

The underlying material is the tent footage itself, slowed and stepped through.
The clip's construction is the analysis: individual frames held, compared, and
narrated.

Three cautions apply to any frame-by-frame argument of this kind, and they apply
here regardless of who is making it:

* **Frame rate sets the resolution of the claim.** Consumer video at 30 frames
  per second gives roughly 33 milliseconds between frames. Events at rifle
  velocities happen inside a single frame. An argument that something "did not"
  appear in the frames immediately after impact is bounded by that sampling
  limit, not by physics.
* **Compression is not neutral.** Social-media video is re-encoded repeatedly.
  Fine detail - particularly small, fast-moving, low-contrast material - is
  exactly what compression discards first. Absence of a feature in a
  heavily-compressed copy is weaker evidence than absence in an original.
* **The generation of the copy matters.** Owens describes footage that was
  *shown to her*. The version examined here is a repost of her walkthrough,
  which is at minimum a third-generation copy of whatever she was working from.

None of that disposes of her observations. It sets the standard at which they
would have to be tested - against an original-generation file, with known frame
timing, ideally alongside the sealed forensic material the site tracks at
[Ballistics Sealed and CBLA](/CoverUp/Ballistics_Sealed_CBLA). What this clip
establishes for certain is that a widely-heard public figure examined the
available footage and reached a stated conclusion that the official round
description does not fit what she saw.

It is worth reading directly beside the other ballistics material on the
[Tyler Robinson](/People/tyler-robinson) page - in particular the NEWSMAX
segment in which Rob O'Neill, asked whether the round could have been stopped as
described, answers "No. No. That's not what happened." Two people with very
different backgrounds arriving at the same objection from different starting
points is the strongest form this argument currently takes.
