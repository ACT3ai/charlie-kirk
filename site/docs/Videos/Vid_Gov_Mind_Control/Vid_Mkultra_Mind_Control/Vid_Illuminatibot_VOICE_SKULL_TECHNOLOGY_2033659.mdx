## What The Footage Shows

A lecture hall, tiered seating, a black circular transducer disc on a metal
stand, an amplifier on a cart, a chalkboard and a periodic table on the wall.
Roughly the first 1:07 is the lecturer explaining with his hands. At 1:07 he
bends to the cart and starts the audio. From 1:29 he lifts the disc off its
stand and sweeps it across the room, and the edit cuts repeatedly to students
who laugh, flinch, touch their heads or duck as the beam crosses them. From
about 2:09 he angles the disc at a wall to show the beam reflecting, and the
clip ends on students raising hands to confirm they can hear the reflection.

What the footage does not show is anything covert, anything military, or
anything that bypasses the ear. The device is a **loudspeaker**. It puts
ultrasound into the air, air's non-linearity demodulates it into audible sound
along the beam path, and the students' ears then hear that sound in the normal
way. The perception that the sound is "coming from your head" is a localisation
illusion - the listener cannot locate a source because the audible sound is
being generated in the air column immediately in front of them, not radiated
from a cone.

"Voice to skull", as the term is used in the material this clip is filed
alongside, means something categorically different: pulsed microwave energy
producing an auditory sensation with no acoustic wave present at all. The
lecturer never claims that. He says 65 kilohertz - an ultrasonic frequency, and
one that will not pass through a wall, a car roof or a skull. The headline on
the repost is the reposter's, not the lecturer's, and the gap between them is
the single most useful thing about this page.
