## What This Video Is

A 2 minute 20 second file held in the investigation's capture archive as
`Professor_Example.mp4`. It carries **no source URL, no poster, and no
headline** - which makes it the cleanest of the three copies of this recording

The recording itself is identifiable from what is stencilled on the

## What Is Said

The lecturer speaks throughout; there is no narrator and no second voice.

He describes the ultrasonic carrier - "it's gonna blast out a 65 kilohertz
beam. Because the wavelength is so small, it doesn't spread" - and compares its
directionality to a laser.

He describes the modulation that makes it audible, then the non-linear
acoustics that make the trick possible only at high sound pressure: "Sound plus
sound can create sound, but only at high amplitude. At low amplitude, sound
waves just travel right through each other."

At roughly 1:41 he states the effect that every repost seizes on: "the sound
that you're going to hear is actually created in the beam, not here. And
because it's created in the beam, since it stays in the beam, we have a beam of
sound... it almost sounds like it's coming from your head."

The remainder is classroom material: reassurance about safety ("It won't hurt
you, we don't think"), choosing a music track ("The Eagles"), the price of the
unit ("It's worth the $2,000"), sweeping the beam over the room ("let me try to
scan the whole audience just so everybody gets to hear this. Absolutely
remarkable"), and finally bouncing it off a wall - "Raise your hand if you can
very clearly hear it."

## What The Footage Shows

A university lecture hall, about fifteen to twenty students in tiered seating,
a black circular acoustic transmitter disc on a metal stand, an amplifier, and
a colleague seated at the demonstration desk. The lecturer explains for the
first minute, starts the audio at 1:07, detaches the disc at 1:29, and from
1:34 onward the edit alternates between him aiming the disc and students
reacting - a young man in a green jacket smiling in surprise, others laughing,
ducking or covering their ears as the beam crosses them. The final twenty
seconds are the wall-reflection demonstration.

Because this copy carries no caption, the footage is easy to read on its own
terms, and on its own terms it shows something specific and limited: a
commercially available directional loudspeaker, operated in the open, in a
lit classroom, by a named institution's physics department, in front of an
audience who can see the device pointing at them. Every element of the
demonstration depends on the sound existing as a pressure wave in the air -
including the reflection off the wall, which could not happen otherwise.

That is not a small finding for this cluster. It means the recording is
evidence that **beamed, head-localised audio is real and buyable**, and it is
not evidence for the separate claim that voices can be induced in a person's
head at a distance with no acoustic wave present. The investigation is better
served by holding those two statements apart than by letting a caption merge
them.
