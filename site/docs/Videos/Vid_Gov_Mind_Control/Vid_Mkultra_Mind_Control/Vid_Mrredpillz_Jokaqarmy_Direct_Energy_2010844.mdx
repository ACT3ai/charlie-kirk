## What This Video Is

A 1 minute 4 second clip, reposted on X by the accounts **@mrredpillz** and
**@jokaqarmy** under the caption "Direct Energy Weapons were used in Venezuela".
It is C-SPAN-style coverage of a **US congressional hearing** on Anomalous
Health Incidents (AHIs) - the official term for what is popularly called Havana
Syndrome. The footage is public-record testimony, re-cut into a short vertical
clip with added text overlays, a low synthesiser music bed, and a "TUPACABRA"
watermark.

Four people are identifiable from the nameplates on screen: **LTC Edgreen**,
**Mr. Grozev** and **Mr. Zaid** at the witness table, and **Mr. Crane** on the
dais asking the questions. The witness panel matches the publicly reported AHI
hearing testimony referenced elsewhere in this investigation, where a retired
Army lieutenant colonel is cited as having told a congressional hearing that
these incidents were affecting personnel across the intelligence community.

The caption should be treated separately from the footage. Nothing in the 64
seconds of testimony mentions **Venezuela**, and nothing in it references the
event the overlay is pointing at. The overlay is the reposter's editorial frame.

## What Is Said

Three exchanges, in order.

The clip opens mid-answer, with the witness describing the effect profile of the
weapons under discussion. This is the most quoted line in the clip:

> "There's no entry or exit wound. How they're designed is to make the target
> feel like they're crazy, like they're imagining things, especially on the low
> duration, the low intensity, long duration hits."

That statement matters because it describes an alleged design intent - producing
symptoms that read as mental illness - rather than a physical injury signature.
It is the mechanism by which, on this account, victims are disbelieved.

At roughly 00:16, Representative Crane asks whether any known AHI sufferers have
died of their symptoms. Both witnesses answer in the affirmative, briefly and
without elaboration: "I do. I do."

At roughly 00:29, Crane asks whether any of the three panellists has personally
seen one of these devices. Grozev answers:

> "I have seen a 1991 version of the weapon, and it looks like a satellite dish
> with a unit this size attached to it."

He then addresses portability, which is the substantive technical content of the
clip:

> "Which, of course, over the years, miniaturisation has been possible.
> Obviously, there's a limitation to how miniaturised it can be because of the
> antenna size, which is always related to the wave, but still, it is something
> that can be well contained in the trunk of a car or even a large backpack."

That is a specific, falsifiable, physics-anchored claim: antenna size is bounded
by wavelength, so the device cannot shrink indefinitely, but a 1991-generation
unit's successor could fit in a car boot or a large rucksack.

## What The Footage Shows

A formal hearing room - wood panelling, a curved dais, US flags, spectators in
rows behind the witness table. Standard broadcast coverage: static medium shots
of the panel, static close-ups of whichever person is speaking, and one wide
establishing shot at about 00:12. Paper cups and bottled water on the table. One
witness, Zaid, never speaks in the clip and simply listens.

The added layer is text: "Direct Energy Weapons were used in Venezuela" and
"cognitive electromagnetic warfare". Neither phrase is spoken by anyone on
camera. The hearing footage is genuine and the testimony is on the record; the
Venezuela framing is bolted on afterwards and is not supported by anything in
the clip's own audio.

There is a second, more useful tension worth recording. Grozev's testimony
describes a **portable, line-of-sight emitter** - satellite dish, car boot,
backpack. That is precisely the model that Jesse Beltran, in the
[Redacted interview](/Videos/Vid_Gov_Mind_Control/Vid_Mkultra_Mind_Control/Vid_Redacted_Havana_Syndrome_Real_2035096)

for a satellite and cell-tower network. One of those two accounts is sworn
congressional testimony and one is a podcast interview relaying an unnamed
source. They cannot both be the whole picture, and the investigation is better
off noting the conflict than filing both as mutually reinforcing.
