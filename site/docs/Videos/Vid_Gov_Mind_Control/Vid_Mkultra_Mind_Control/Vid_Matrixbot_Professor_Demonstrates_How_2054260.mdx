The three transcripts of the three copies differ only in speech-recognition

than three independent demonstrations. This page exists because the clip is

summary is that the investigation has one demo, not three data points.

## What Is Said

One voice, the lecturer's, talking to a class about parametric acoustics.

He introduces an inaudible carrier: "When I do, it's gonna blast out a 65
kilohertz beam. Because the wavelength is so small, it doesn't spread... similar
to a laser, it just comes right out as a beam."

He explains how the carrier becomes audible: "they put a modulation on top of
the 65 kilohertz", and then why that only works at high power - "Sound plus
sound can create sound, but only at high amplitude. At low amplitude, sound
waves just travel right through each other."

The headline claim comes from one sentence around 1:41: "the sound that you're
going to hear is actually created in the beam, not here... it almost sounds like
it's coming from your head."

He also states a caution the reposts strip out: "It won't hurt you. We don't
think. You certainly can't hear it, and we don't think it'll hurt you." He
values the unit at "$2,000" and closes by asking the class to "raise your hand
if you can very clearly hear it" while he reflects the beam off a wall.

## What The Footage Shows

* **00:00 - 01:07** - The lecturer, mid-50s to 60s, in a light button-down and
  khakis, explaining beside a black circular transducer disc on a stand. Static
  camera.
* **01:07 - 01:30** - He bends over a cart and starts the audio.
* **01:30 - 01:34** - He lifts the disc off the stand and aims it at the room.
* **01:35 - 02:08** - Repeated cuts between the lecturer sweeping the disc and
  students reacting: laughter, a young man touching his head, others ducking or
  covering their ears as the beam passes.
* **02:09 - 02:19** - He angles the disc at a side wall to demonstrate
  reflection; students in the reflected path raise their hands.

Two features of the frame do more analytical work than the headline. First, the
students react **in sequence**, as the disc sweeps - which is exactly what an
airborne directional beam does and exactly what a through-wall or
through-skull mechanism would not need. Second, the reflection demonstration
only works because the sound is a physical pressure wave in the room that can
bounce off plaster. Both observations point the same way: this is a
loudspeaker with a very narrow polar pattern, not an emitter that writes sound
directly into a nervous system.

"Silently beamed into your head", the phrase in the repost's headline, is
therefore doing two jobs at once. It is a fair description of the listener's
*experience* - the sound genuinely is not locatable and genuinely does feel
internal. It is not a fair description of the *mechanism*. Anyone using this
clip to support a microwave voice-to-skull claim is relying on that ambiguity.
