## What This Video Is

A 6 minute compilation posted to X by the account @UAPLuigi, assembled from
older broadcast documentary material. It is not footage from this case and makes
no reference to it. It is background: an explainer on three related technologies
that get invoked whenever an unexplained sound or sensation enters an
investigation.

Its value is that the demonstrations are real, filmed, and attributed to named
researchers on camera, which is unusual for material in this genre. Its limit is
equally clear: nothing in it is evidence about anything that happened in Utah,
and the clip does not claim otherwise.

## What Is Said

Three segments, three speakers.

The first is a physics lecture demonstration of a directional ultrasonic
speaker. The lecturer explains the principle before switching it on: "it's going
to blast out a 65 kilohertz beam. Because the wavelength is so small, it doesn't
spread ... similar to a laser. It just comes right out as a beam." He explains
that the audible content is carried as a modulation on that carrier, notes the
carrier is inaudible and high amplitude, and gives the mechanism: "sound plus
sound can create sound, but only at high amplitude. At low amplitude sound waves
just travel right through each other." The consequence is the striking part:
"the sound that you're going to hear is actually created in the beam, not here
... it almost sounds like it's coming from your head." He then scans the room
and bounces the beam off the back wall, asking students to raise a hand if they
hear it. Many do.

The second is archival news reporting on the microwave auditory effect, filmed
in an anechoic chamber in Chicago with the biomedical engineer Professor James
Lin as the subject. Lin describes what he perceives as pulses of microwave
energy are fired at him: "I'm hearing a micro-pulse, like a click. Now it sounds
like a chirp, with a tonal quality to it." The narrator gives the accepted
physical explanation: absorbed microwave energy heats brain tissue very slightly,
it expands, and the resulting pressure wave is picked up by the inner ear. Lin is
careful about the limits, and the narration is careful with him: he is described
as "far from hearing voices". Asked about encoding, he answers in principle
rather than in fact -- the brain is an electrical organ, microwave signals are
electrical, so "in principle, one could embed or encode information in the
microwave signal."

The third is a practical application of the first: a researcher on a library
mezzanine using a directional dish to speak to individual readers -- "hey, you
there, you won the laptop" -- while people sitting beside them hear nothing. He
explains the genuine use case, which is delivering audio in a space where
background noise is unacceptable.

The last segment is the one that needs the most care. Dr Michael Persinger,
filmed in his laboratory, describes electromagnetic stimulation of subjects and
says that appropriate fields can induce relaxation, and equally "we can induce
fear and apprehension, but clearly that would be unethical in that setting." He
then extrapolates a long way: "if you understand how they work, you can make
entire populations think and decide the manner in which you wish." The
programme's own narrator flags the pushback, noting that many experts are
sceptical of that scenario, and the segment closes on speculative framing about
future conflict. That extrapolation is Persinger's, it is contested, and this
site does not adopt it.

## What The Footage Shows

A lecture hall with a periodic table on the wall and a flat black disc on a
stand; students reacting, laughing and covering their ears as the beam sweeps
across them; hands going up when it is bounced off the wall. Then red-tinted
graphics of transmission towers, a night aerial of Chicago, and a dark anechoic
chamber with acoustic foam, an oscilloscope showing pulses and a blue horn
antenna behind the subject's head. Then a bright library with split-screen
reaction shots of startled readers. Then a dim green-lit laboratory with a
rack-mounted console, a paper EEG chart, and a subject in a headband with leads
attached, followed by crowd b-roll.

The demonstrations are filmed and repeatable. What is shown is that a beam of
audible sound can be aimed at one person in a crowd, and that pulsed microwave
energy can produce clicks and chirps a subject perceives as sound. What is not
shown, anywhere, is a device projecting intelligible speech into a specific
person's head at range, or any application of any of this to a real event. The
distance between the first claim and the second is the whole question, and the
video does not close it.
