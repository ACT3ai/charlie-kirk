## What Is Said

Only the question that was in the air when the recording cuts. The transcript returns the same fragment six times, once per repetition:

> In America, over the last 10 years, counting or not counting gang violence.

Followed each time by a single word of acknowledgement. That is the questioner mid-sentence at the "Prove Me Wrong" table, and it survives on the tape because the edit keeps rewinding past it. Investigation notes record a witness independently remembering the same thing — that Kirk "was talking about gang violence and then I'd just hear like a loud pop." The clip therefore fixes, in audio, what the exchange was about at the instant everything stopped. That is a small but genuinely useful anchor for anyone reconstructing the last minute.

There is no narration in the clip, and no analysis in words. Everything the edit argues, it argues by repetition.

The reason this edit exists is the slow-motion passes at 00:29 and 00:43, and specifically the movement of the shirt fabric. Researchers examining this case have argued that the cloth moves outward and upward, away from the body, and that a metal clasp is thrown upward — a pattern they say is inconsistent with a rifle round and consistent with pressure escaping from something worn on the chest. That is the argument the repetition is built to let a viewer see for themselves, and this clip is one of the primary objects it is made from.

It is an argument, not a finding.
