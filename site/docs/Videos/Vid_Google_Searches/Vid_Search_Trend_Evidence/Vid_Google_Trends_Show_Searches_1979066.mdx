## What The Footage Shows

The exhibits appear in sequence behind the presenter: mugshot images captioned "Tyler James Robinson"; a Google Trends chart for `tyler james robinson` filtered to the Washington DC area, with the large September 12 spike and a much smaller September 9 bump; a Google Earth satellite view with the Losee Center marked as the claimed vantage point; a Trends chart for `losee center` with a bump at September 8; a side-by-side of Robinson and a Mauser 98 rifle; a Trends chart for `mauser 98` with a rise at August 27; a screenshot of a post on X claiming the data was scrubbed; and finally a Google Trends page displaying an error message reporting insufficient data for the query.

Three cautions belong on the page, because the charts on screen do not carry the weight the framing puts on them.

The Trends index is relative, not a count. When the presenter's own chart contains a September 12 spike normalised to 100 — the day the name went worldwide — every earlier point on that chart is being measured against a national news event. A "small bump" beside that peak can represent an extremely small number of queries, and at low volumes Google's sampling and rounding make single-day bumps unstable. Re-running the same query on a different day can produce a different small bump.

The last exhibit is not what it is presented as. A Trends page showing "insufficient data" is the normal output for a low-volume query in a narrow region, and it is also what you get when the region, the date range or the category filter differs even slightly from the earlier run. It is consistent with data being removed. It is equally consistent with a different query. The video does not show the two queries side by side with identical filters, which is what would distinguish them. The site treats this whole question separately on [Trends deletion and suppression claims](/GoogleSearches/trends-deletion-suppression).

And nothing in any of these charts identifies a searcher. A metro-area label reflects the network the request came from, not a person, and Tyler Robinson is a common name.
