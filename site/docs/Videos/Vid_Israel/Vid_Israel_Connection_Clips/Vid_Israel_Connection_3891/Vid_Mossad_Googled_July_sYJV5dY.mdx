## What This Video Is

Six minutes and eleven seconds of commentary and screen-share, alternating between a presenter talking to camera in an office and full-screen captures of the Google Trends interface. On-screen furniture identifies the presenter as Sam Parker, with watermarks for `@BasedSamParker` and `@SamParkerSenate`, under a burned-in headline reading "BOMBSHELL: ISRAELI AND WASHINGTON DC IP ADDRESSES CONDUCTED UNIQUE GOOGLE SEARCHES." The archive holds it as `Mossad_Googled_In_July.mp4` with no posting account recorded. It has not been published to IPFS, so a still frame stands in for the player.

Two things should be fixed in the reader's mind before the summary below.

First: every person Parker names in this clip is a private medical professional, and he does not accuse any of them of anything. Neither does this page. The claim under discussion is about who was searching for their names, not about them. Being the subject of a web search is not conduct, and implies nothing whatever about the person searched for.

Second: everything here is Parker's reading of a public aggregate tool. Google Trends reports relative interest, not individual queries; its geographic attribution reflects where a request appeared to originate, which a VPN or proxy will change; and low-volume terms are noisy by construction. Parker himself flags the VPN problem on camera. The clip raises a question. It does not close one.

## What Is Said

Parker narrates continuously. The structure is a chain of searches, each with a date.

He starts with the hospital: "I wonder if anybody Googled the hospital where they took Charlie Kirk." He describes finding a spike in interest in Timpanogos Regional Hospital on 10 and 11 September, weighted to the District of Columbia, which he treats as unremarkable — that is people looking up where the casualty was taken. What he presents as the anomaly is what sits before it: a quiet two months, and then a cluster around what he repeatedly calls "that July 20 anchor date," with hits he reads as 16 July, 3 July and 28 June. Then, he says, one more the day before the shooting: "Somebody in the District of Columbia says, we should really make sure we know what the Timpanogos Regional Hospital is... the day before he gets shot."

He is careful, on camera, about how far he will push it: "I'm just reporting the facts. I am not drawing conclusions tonight. I could give you possible conclusions you could draw, and you could draw your own conclusion, or you could just sit back and look and learn like I did."

His second thread is the medical examiner. He explains why he went looking — "an autopsy could give you a lot of things, such as what angle did the bullet come in? Where did the bullet go? What type of bullet was it?" — and states his understanding, hedged, that no autopsy was performed: "it appears to me, based on everything I've looked at, that Charlie Kirk may not have had an autopsy, which I find shocking." He then reports a search for the Utah chief medical examiner, Deirdre Amaro, which he says shows on 30 June 2025 from an IP address originating in Israel, and which he says does not show for the District of Columbia or for Utah. His inference from that absence is only that the term is rare: "This is not a very common search... not a very common search at all."

His third thread is the surgeons. Prompted, he says, by a TPUSA spokesperson relaying a surgeon's account without naming the surgeon, Parker says he compiled a list of surgeons practising at the Orem facility and checked each name. He reports, with dates he reads off the interface: Steven Neelman on 18 July; Robert Patterson MD on 23 and 24 July, which he notes is the only one searched on two consecutive days; Lee Trotter around the same period; Lee Patterson on 19 July; Brian Gill on 22 July; Richard Rasmussen on 14 July. He attributes each to an IP address originating in Israel. His summary of the pattern is a two-week window: "within a very narrow time window, like seven to ten days of each other."

His ask is investigative rather than accusatory, and he addresses it to agencies: "If I'm the Utah State Police, I'm calling Google and saying... I need to know if this belongs to a VPN. Is it the same IP address?" He closes by naming the question he thinks that would answer: "I want to know who the hell is so curious about my surgeons at my local hospital."

Investigation notes hold a broadly matching list of names and dates around 20 July, with one additional name not spoken in this clip. That means the claim has been recorded elsewhere in the same form; it does not mean it has been independently verified, and the underlying Trends views are not preserved on this page.

## What The Footage Shows

The clip alternates about a dozen times between two things: Parker at eye level in his office, gesturing, and a browser window showing the Google Trends interface. The screen captures are the evidence he is pointing at — a hospital query filtered to the District of Columbia at 00:07, an examiner query attributed to Israel at 01:50, then one surgeon name after another between 03:34 and 05:44.

What the footage shows, strictly, is a man operating a public web tool and reading the results aloud. It does not show query logs, IP records, or anything from Google itself. Nothing on screen distinguishes a genuine origin from a proxy. The visual evidence is a screenshot of a chart, and a screenshot of a chart is exactly as strong as the tool behind it — which, for rare terms, is not very strong. That is a limitation of the method, not a criticism of the presenter, and he acknowledges most of it himself.
