A 7 minute 19 second document walkthrough, shared to X by @Bechedith on 12 July 2026 as a reply to @baroncoleman and @VLuvMully. The speaker on camera is Baron Coleman, an attorney and radio commentator who has covered this case since early in the year.

Format matters here. This is not raw footage of anything. It is a screen-share podcast: Coleman on the left of a split screen in a home studio, a PDF on the right, his cursor highlighting lines as he reads them. Everything the clip asserts is an inference drawn from two documents on screen. That is a strength - the documents are visible and checkable - and a limit, because the argument stands or falls on how those documents are read.

The claims below are Coleman's, attributed to him and not adopted here. Tyler Robinson is charged and has not been convicted of anything.

## What Is Said

Coleman works through two exhibits in order.

The first is a defence motion - he identifies it on screen as a motion to exclude still photographers, television cameras and microphones - which quotes a video of an interview at Bates stamp 003996-R2. He reads the quoted timestamp aloud: "a video of the encounter at Bates 3996 R2... at this timestamp of the video indicates the following, and this is 6:25 p.m." The quoted content is a rights advisement: "before we begin, I'm just going to read you your rights... You have the right to remain silent."

His central observation is what the document does not carry: "what we don't get in this document, we don't get a date."

His argument for the date is built from two lines in the quoted transcript. First, the response: "I'd like to speak with my legal counsel. I'm hoping for a fellow named Doug Terry... I think my folks are trying to get a hold of him. I think his office is closed for the night." Coleman reads "closed for the night" as a weekday, not a weekend: "if this was on 9/12 in Utah County as the fed sloppers want us to believe, then this would be closed for the weekend, not for the night." Second, he reads "my folks are trying to get a hold of him" as indicating recent contact with family.

The second exhibit is a Utah County Sheriff's Office inmate booking sheet and probable cause statement. Coleman reads an arrest date of 9/11/2025 at 10 p.m. and an arresting officer named Brian Davis, and states that Davis is an officer with the State Bureau of Investigation. He then runs a travel-time argument: a drive of roughly three and a half hours from the northern part of the state, meaning a departure around 6:30 p.m. to make a 10 p.m. arrest, which he says "matches up nearly to the minute of when I suggested he was being mirandized." He notes the booked-in time in Utah County is four hours after the arrest, and asks how the outbound leg could have taken less. His characterisation of the response he gets is "the fed sloppers have no answer for this. They just ignore it."

He closes on the consequence he draws. If the rights advisement was at 6:25 p.m. on September 11, then in his account it precedes the Discord messages by about ninety minutes, and he finds it hard to reconcile a person declining to answer questions with that person then messaging a confession: "I'm to believe while he's declining to answer any questions, an hour and a half later, he picks up a phone, gets on Discord and says, oh, I did it, guys."

Every step of that is an inference from documents, offered by an advocate. "Closed for the night" is capable of more than one reading. Travel-time estimates depend on route, mode and traffic. Coleman states his conclusion with certainty; the documents on screen do not carry a date, which is precisely why the dispute exists.
