## What Is Said

Owens states her own conclusions early and plainly, which at least makes her frame legible: "I do not believe Tyler Robinson assassinated Charlie Kirk, and I believe that Turning Point USA is implicated in this moment in a cover-up of sorts." Those are her beliefs, stated as such.

The substantive part - the part worth the eight minutes - is her account of the discovery argument. As she describes it, the defence asked to see underlying data and the state's answer was a summary. On the firearm: "they're saying they have a screen report on the gun. That's just a summary report that the FBI gave to the defence. The defence is saying, okay, now can we see the digitized files? Can we see the fingerprints? Can we see your process and how you got this? No. You just get the summary." She frames the defence position as a question about testability: "we need to make sure that what you're saying you saw and what you have is real."

She makes the same point about chain of custody: "the defence is saying they won't give us the chain of custody. So basically, we can't dispute anything they're saying." She extends it to the timeline evidence, saying that without the files the defence cannot test when images were captured, and characterises the remaining basis as "a report that says, well, Tyler Robinson, the FBI concluded, jumped onto the rooftop at 12:19 p.m."

Her second thread is procedural and is about the continuance. She says both sides agree the ATF and FBI have not handed over data files held since September, and she questions why the prosecution opposes a delay: "I have never been in a case where both sides don't just agree to a general continuance." She connects that to a public statement about a speedy trial and says it is the reason she reads the posture as strategic.

She then names living people and states opinions about them, and those are hers alone. She says of Erika Kirk that she reads the position as "a slight of hand" and states "I find her to be malevolent, and that's all I'll say about that." She says Donald Trump, JD Vance and Kash Patel "are allowing this charade to go on," and asks why they do not direct the files to be produced. Those are characterisations offered by a commentator; no wrongdoing by any of them is established, and Erika Kirk's stated position, as Owens herself reports it, is that she wants the proceeding to move forward promptly.

She closes on a comparison rather than a claim: "never did I think that we would be alive for something like JFK."

The part of this worth carrying forward is the narrow procedural point, which does not depend on believing any of her conclusions: a defence that receives a conclusion without the data behind it cannot test the conclusion. That question is live in the filings regardless of what anyone thinks of the speaker.
