## What Is Said

She opens by quoting the message that prompted her, and it is important to keep
the two apart. What she was sent asserts flatly that Charlie Kirk was killed
with a miniature explosive made by a Tennessee manufacturer and bought by the
Department of Defense, and names a senior serving defence official as having
been involved in planning the killing. She does not adopt that. She goes and
pulls the records instead.

**What she found in the records.** She says she pulled all of the company's
contracts from USASpending.gov and isolated one:

* Line item described as **miniaturized-XS demolition devices, anti-personnel**.
* Amount **$440,494**.
* Performance period **May 2025 to August 2025**.
* Contract signed **April 22, 2025**.

**The four anomalies she flags**, and these are procurement-process
observations rather than claims about anyone's conduct:

1. **Duration.** She says none of the company's other awards ran less than a
   year, and that a three-month period of performance is unusual for this kind
   of item.
2. **Simplified acquisition.** The award went through a simplified acquisition
   process — in her words, "did it quickly."
3. **Single bidder.** One bidder rather than a competed field.
4. **Off-vehicle.** It was not placed under any of the indefinite-delivery
   vehicles the company holds for its much larger routine business.

**What she says the item is.** She characterises miniaturised extra-small
anti-personnel charges as items made for special operations forces, small enough
to be placed on a person.

**The ownership point.** She notes a corporate connection between a private
equity firm previously led by a senior defence official and a company that
trains special operations personnel on detonation devices, and she comments on
that official's stated policy priorities. That connection is her observation. It
establishes no link to the events of September 10, 2025, and none is asserted
here. The official named in the message she received is a living public
figure who has not been charged with, accused by any authority of, or found by
any process to have had any involvement in the death of Charlie Kirk. This page
does not repeat that accusation as anything but a claim made in a message she
was forwarded.

**The plant explosion.** She adds that the facility at the contract address
exploded roughly a month after the assassination and that workers there were
killed. That event is documented separately on this site.

**Her own conclusion, stated explicitly:**

> Correlation doesn't mean causality. I'm a big, big advocate of that. However,
> something doesn't smell right at all. So if someone knows more, or can help me
> correct some of this or figure out what's going on, let me know.

That is the correct posture for the material, and it is worth crediting: the
speaker states her methodology, publishes the record she worked from, marks
where she is inferring, disclaims causation, and asks to be corrected. The value
of the clip is the four procurement anomalies, each of which is checkable against
the primary award record. The value is not the framing she was sent.

Two accuracy notes for anyone citing this. First, the figure circulating in
secondary posts varies between roughly $425,000 and $440,494; the award record
is the authority, and the corroborated figure on this site is **$440,494**.
Second, an unusual procurement is not evidence of an unusual purpose. Short
performance periods, simplified acquisitions and single-bidder awards happen
routinely for small, specialised orders where one qualified supplier exists.
Each anomaly she lists has an ordinary explanation available; the question the
material raises is whether all four together, on this item, in this window,
warrant an answer. That is a question, and this site treats it as one.
