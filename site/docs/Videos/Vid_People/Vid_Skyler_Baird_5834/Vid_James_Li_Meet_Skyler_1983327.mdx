## What Is Said

Li opens with a line about the case as a whole: "The gang of interesting characters surrounding the death of Charlie Kirk continues to grow."

He then plays Baird's broadcast interview, in which Baird says he was "maybe 10 or 15 feet away when it happened" and is asked by an anchor how he wants Kirk remembered. Li's assessment of that footage is, notably, sympathetic rather than accusatory:

> That was one Skyler Baird, with what I think is actually quite a completely natural reaction to witnessing somebody get publicly executed on that tragic day on September the 10th.

That is worth quoting precisely because the surrounding commentary ecosystem does the opposite. Li explicitly declines to read the emotional display as fake. His argument is placed elsewhere: "but that is just about the only thing that is normal in this story, as things about Skyler will only get weirder and weirder as we progress in this investigation."

The substance he offers in this short clip is a single point, and it is a real one. He plays Baird describing his own first action after the shooting:

> I was right there and kind of escorted him. I mean, there was a cop right there too, but kind of escorted this guy to the cop, and he was like, I shot him, I shot him.

Li identifies the man being described as George Zinn — "the guy who stood up as soon as Charlie was shot and shouted, shoot me, shoot me" — and notes what followed publicly: "then after that, we were told he was some kind of bad, bad man with very dark proclivities. So let's forget all about him."

His conclusion is put as a raised eyebrow rather than a charge: "Well, I guess Skyler just happened to be there to help apprehend the decoy. Coincidence, I suppose. So this naturally leads the internet to do its thing and start internetting."

Three things should be separated out of that.

The factual core — that Baird has described directing an officer toward Zinn — comes from Baird's own on-camera account and is not in dispute; Baird repeats it himself in the longer interview and argues that it cuts against, not for, the theory built on it.

The word "decoy" is Li's characterisation. Zinn's role, and whether he functioned as a distraction, is a contested question handled elsewhere on this site; calling him a decoy assumes the answer.

And the closing line concedes the point about method: what Li is describing is the internet doing its thing, which is how most of the material about this witness came to exist. He is narrating a phenomenon at least as much as making a case.
