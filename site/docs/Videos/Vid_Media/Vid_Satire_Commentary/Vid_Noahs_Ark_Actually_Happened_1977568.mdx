## What Is Said

The performer plays the accused in the minutes after the shooting, panting,
pretending to type a confession into a phone. Cleaned up, the monologue runs:

> Oh, God. I can't believe I just did that. I should probably get the heck out

>

> and a 34 on the ACT, is smart enough to know that there is no way that the FBI

The sketch also has the character addressing his message to his roommate, in
mocking terms this page does not repeat. [Lance Twiggs](/People/lance-twiggs) is
a living person and is not accused of anything here.

The joke is an argument, and it is worth stating in plain terms what the
argument is, because that is the only reason this clip is in an evidence
archive. The sketch mocks the internal coherence of the account: that a person
who planned for a week, engraved rounds, and successfully left the scene would
then immediately commit a detailed written confession to a traceable messaging
platform — and that the same person's academic record is offered as evidence of
his capability while the confession is offered as evidence of his carelessness.
It compresses into sixty seconds the tension between "meticulous planner" and
"instantly self-incriminating," which is the substantive objection raised at
length across the pages linked below.

Being funny is not being right. Ridicule proves nothing, and every element the
sketch mocks has an ordinary explanation available: people who commit serious
crimes routinely behave inconsistently, panic, and confess; academic ability and
operational discipline are unrelated; and the rifle-assembly detail in the
sketch is an exaggeration of the record rather than a quotation of it. The
messages themselves, the custody timing around them, and the disputes over how
they were produced are examined properly elsewhere on this site.
[Tyler Robinson](/People/tyler-robinson) is a living person who has pleaded not
guilty; nothing on this page asserts that he committed any offence.
