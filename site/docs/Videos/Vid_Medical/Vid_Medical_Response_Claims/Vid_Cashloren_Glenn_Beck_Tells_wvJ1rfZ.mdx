## What Is Said

The whole exchange, as transcribed:

> **Kelly:** Glenn, start with you. Thank you for being here. I just read your tweet to the audience. Are you hearing that from people who are in a position to know — that Charlie is alive, and that he's in critical?
>
> **Beck:** I am. I spoke to somebody in a position who knows, who said he has a pulse. So that's good news. Good news.

That is all of it. It is worth reading closely for what it does and does not establish.

It is a broadcaster relaying, live and at second hand, what an unnamed source told him. He identifies no one, gives no time for the observation, and does not say whether the source was at the scene, at the hospital, or relaying it themselves from someone else. He frames it, plainly, as good news — a man on air hoping out loud.

What it does document precisely is a **timestamp on the information**: at 20:07 UTC on September 10, a nationally broadcast programme was reporting, on the strength of an insider source, that Charlie Kirk was alive with a pulse. Kirk's death was confirmed publicly later. The gap between the two is the whole reason this clip matters. It is one of the anchor points for reconstructing what was believed, and by whom, in the first hours.

This page attributes no fault to anyone. Reporting a source's account in real time during a mass-casualty event is ordinary journalism, and being overtaken by events is the normal hazard of it. The clip is evidence about the flow of information, not about anyone's conduct.
