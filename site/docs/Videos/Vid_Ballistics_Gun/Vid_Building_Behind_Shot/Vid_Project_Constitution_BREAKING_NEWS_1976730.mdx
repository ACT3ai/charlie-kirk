## What This Video Is

Twenty-one minutes thirty-one seconds, published by the account Project Constitution under a headline announcing that the real killer had been caught on camera. It is a narrated frame-by-frame analysis, not footage: the presenter plays event video inside an editing view, pauses, zooms, adds arrows, and talks continuously.

The presenter says he is ex-military and credits a named collaborator he describes as ex-CIA for part of the material. He also states repeatedly that he is deliberately blurring frames and keeping the sound off to avoid platform enforcement - "I can't show you guys anything gruesome because they'll ban me" - which means the imagery a viewer is asked to interpret has been degraded twice over, first by compression and then by the presenter's own blurring.

Before any of it is summarised, the frame must be set. What follows is a report of what this video asserts. None of it is adopted by this site. The video makes serious accusations about unnamed private individuals who were seated in the audience at a public event. Those accusations are unproven and uncorroborated, no one has been charged with or found responsible for anything the video alleges, and no individual is named or identified on this page.

## What Is Said

**The sniper-position claim.** The presenter opens by reasserting a shooting position he has argued for before: "I said that the sniper actually shot from right here, okay? And I meant exactly what I said." He then walks through what he presents as three corroborating artefacts in the frames - a brief bright point he calls a muzzle flash, a smear he identifies as the bullet in flight, and a line he calls an infrared beam.

Each of those is a few pixels in compressed, blurred video. A brief bright point in such footage is equally compatible with a specular reflection off glass or metal, a camera sensor artefact, or a compression block; the presenter calls it "a reflection" himself at one point and then treats it as a muzzle flash anyway. A smear consistent with a bullet in flight is not something consumer video at ordinary frame rates can resolve. And an apparent beam in a daylight frame is the classic signature of lens flare. The video does not test any of these alternatives.

**The second-shooter claim.** The larger part of the runtime argues that a person seated near the stage holding a mobile phone was in fact signalling, and then firing a concealed weapon. The presenter narrates it as: an arm emerging from behind a phone to give a signal, the person stepping into sunlight so as to be seen, and a small weapon concealed in the hand.

This is an interpretation of ordinary movement. Holding up a phone, adjusting a sleeve, and shifting position are what an audience does. The presenter offers no independent evidence - no identification, no recovered weapon, no forensic finding - and the frames he relies on are the same blurred, magnified frames described above. The people he points at are members of the public who attended a political event. This page will not repeat his identifying descriptions of them and does not treat his reading as established.

**The microphone.** He addresses and rejects one theory circulating elsewhere: "you guys can see clearly the microphone did not blow up. It's still there. These are little magnets that slide off real easy." He then leaves a door open - "maybe it was a detonated device that just didn't go off... I don't know." This is one of the few passages where he argues against a popular claim rather than for one, and it is worth noting that the exploding-microphone thesis is examined on its own terms elsewhere on this site.

**The audio anchor.** At the moment of the shot the underlying event audio surfaces briefly with the same clause that anchors every other angle in this archive - the closing words of the final question about how mass shootings are counted. That confirms the video is working from the genuine event recording rather than from a reconstruction.

## What The Footage Shows

On screen: an editing timeline, arrows and circles drawn over heavily magnified and deliberately blurred frames, slow-motion loops replayed many times, and the presenter's continuous narration. Blurring is applied over the injury throughout.

The method is the problem, and it is worth naming because it recurs across a great deal of the material in this section. Take a compressed video, magnify a region far past its resolution, replay the resulting artefacts in a loop while narrating what they are, and the viewer will see what they are told to see. That is not a criticism of the presenter's sincerity; it is a statement about what the source can and cannot support. Nothing at this magnification distinguishes a muzzle flash from a reflection, or a person adjusting a sleeve from anything else.
