## What This Video Is

Forty-three minutes twenty-eight seconds of stage video: Tucker Carlson's full speech and audience question session at the Turning Point USA Student Action Summit in Tampa, Florida, in July 2025. It is a produced multi-camera capture with a TCN end card, not a phone recording. Carlson walks out through spark fountains, speaks from a clear acrylic podium against a magenta Turning Point backdrop, and takes two questions from the floor at the end.

The reason a speech about Jeffrey Epstein sits in a Charlie Kirk investigation archive is the venue and the date. This was Kirk's own conference, roughly two months before September 10, and Carlson opens by saying he never misses a Charlie Kirk event. The same summit produced the Kirk and Megyn Kelly conversation and the Israel debate Kirk moderated, both also filed in this section. Whatever weight is given to arguments about motive, this is the documented content of the last major Turning Point gathering before the assassination.

## What Is Said

Carlson's argument is procedural before it is substantive: that the offence is the refusal to answer, not the subject matter. He opens on the summit's dominant topic and disputes a widely circulated dismissal of it, characterising the position as "it happened a long time ago, who cares" and adding that the accompanying instruction is "we should trust the government... and it's wrong to ask a lot of unauthorized questions." His response: "I think that's absurd."

He then generalises the complaint into the theme he returns to throughout. On being answered with an insult rather than an answer: "When you ask a direct question to someone in charge, you are due... that person is morally bound to give you an answer. He's not bound to agree with you, but he's bound to stop and answer your question."

His specific claim about the file releases is that the material may not exist to release, and that this is itself the scandal. About twelve minutes in he states his theory directly: the Department of Justice "didn't release lots of incriminating... videos... because they don't have them," and they do not have them because "when the original search warrant was served" in 2006 or 2007 "it was basically designed to protect Epstein. The search warrant was written in such a way to make sure that the feds never got their hands on the actually incriminating evidence." He draws the conclusion aloud: "It's another way of saying the cover up has been going on since 2007, almost 20 years."

From there he reframes what he says the real question is: "The real question is not, was Jeffrey Epstein a weirdo who was abusing girls, yes, we can answer that. The real question is, why was he doing this, on whose behalf, and where did the money come from?" He asks how a former schoolteacher acquired multiple aircraft, an island and a Manhattan townhouse, and says nobody has answered it "because no one has ever tried."

The most contested passage is where he names the government he believes the question points toward, and he is careful to frame it as a question that citizens are entitled to ask rather than as a finding: "Now, no one's allowed to say that that foreign government is Israel because we have been somehow cowed into thinking that that's naughty. There is nothing wrong with saying that." He puts it as an interrogative: "were you working on behalf of Mossad? Were you running a blackmail operation on behalf of a foreign government?" He immediately concedes the evidentiary position: "if all of that is false, then just tell me how it's false."

He is equally explicit that criticism of an ally is not hostility to a people, drawing the analogy to his own criticism of the CIA: "criticizing the behavior of a government agency does not make you a hater, it makes you a free person."

The last quarter is audience questions. One student presses him on Cuba and hemispheric policy; Carlson answers that a decades-old embargo has not achieved its stated aim and that United States attention is misallocated away from its own hemisphere. A second questioner asks how ordinary supporters are meant to retain trust in institutions if elites are not held to the same standard, and Carlson answers with the line he closes on: "What matters in this country is not what religion, ethnicity, race you are, it's whether or not you're a US citizen... There is nobody, rich, poor, no matter what your religion, what's your color, who's held to a different legal standard."

Everything above is Carlson's stated position at a public event. He offers no documentary exhibit on stage, and several of his central assertions, including the claim about the 2007 warrant's drafting, are presented as his own conclusion from reporting rather than as established fact. This site records the speech; it does not adopt the speech.
