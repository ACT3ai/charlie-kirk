## What This Video Is

One hour nineteen minutes fifty-seven seconds: the complete, unedited multi-camera recording of the debate that closed the Turning Point USA Student Action Summit in Tampa in July 2025. Josh Hammer of Newsweek argues one side, comedian and libertarian commentator Dave Smith the other, and Charlie Kirk moderates from the centre chair.

Kirk fixes the date himself in his opening words: "a year ago today, Donald Trump was in Butler, Pennsylvania," placing the debate on 13 July 2025, roughly two months before his own death. He opens with a moment of silence for Corey Comperatore, the volunteer firefighter killed at Butler.

This is the single most-cited artefact from the summit, because Kirk did not merely host it. He designed it. His framing on stage is explicit: "let's lean into that disagreement... we want to foster an environment where we get to the truth." That editorial choice, staging a genuine argument about Israel policy in front of seven thousand young conservatives, is the reason this file sits in the archive.

## What Is Said

Kirk sets three subjects at the top: the twelve-day war between Israel and Iran and how the administration handled it; the question of Israeli influence in American politics; and, crucially, a demand that both men state a positive proposal rather than only objections.

Smith opens with a structural argument about the last quarter-century of American foreign policy: "American foreign policy for my entire life has been insane... We've launched war after war. We've talked about peace through strength the whole time, but all we've gotten is permanent militarism and forever wars against countries that pose no threat to us." He attributes this to a documented policy current rather than to a hidden hand, citing the 1996 Clean Break memorandum and its companion paper by name, and General Wesley Clark's account of post-9/11 plans to change governments in seven countries.

Hammer's counter-argument is that the influence framing is a distortion. On the lobbying group Smith raises: "APAC is not this nefarious boogeyman that Dave Smith and people make it out to be... It is a group of Americans who place a premium on the U.S. Israel relationship the same way that the NRA is a group of Americans who place a premium on the Second Amendment." He points out that comparable advocacy groups exist for other countries' relationships and are not treated the same way.

The exchange becomes genuinely heated around casualties, with Hammer challenging Smith on whether he has spoken to families of Americans killed in Beirut in 1983 and in Iraq. Kirk intervenes as moderator to slow it: "Why don't we both just take a deep breath? Because they want to see a debate where they can make their mind up." He then restates each man's position back to him before letting them answer, which is the clearest demonstration in the file of what Kirk was doing on that stage.

Smith's positive proposal is fiscal and constitutional rather than personal: "We should be friends with Israel. We should trade with Israel and have a good relationship with them. But... they shouldn't get another dime of U.S. taxpayer money." He closes on debt: "We have spent 20 trillion dollars since the collapse of the Soviet Union. The war in Iraq and Afghanistan are over, and we're looking at our biggest defense budget ever... No Islamist is gonna take down our country. But drowning in debt and empire will."

Kirk's summation is the part most worth reading closely, because it is his own voice recording where he thought the disagreement actually landed. He lists five points of agreement he believes were reached: that both men accept some plan to wind down United States aid to Israel and differ only on timeline; that both hold every person to be made in the image of God and refuse to trivialise deaths; that the October 7 attack was terrible and should be discussed more; that hatred of Jews "has no place in public discourse, period"; and finally that "this is a debate worth having, that it's better to have this out in the open, than silence people, and censor them, and call them names."

Both men are living public figures arguing policy. Nothing said by either is treated here as established fact, and the summary above reports their positions rather than endorsing them.

## What The Footage Shows

A large produced stage. Kirk introduces from the centre; a title card reads "ISRAEL DEBATE / DAVE SMITH & JOSH HAMMER"; the two men walk out through spark fountains, shake hands and sit in white leather armchairs with notepads and bottled water. The bulk of the runtime alternates between a wide three-shot and medium close-ups of whoever is speaking. At the close the three stand, shake hands, embrace and leave the stage to applause.

The camera work matters in one respect only: it is a friendly house recording. There are no cutaways to hostile audience reaction and no editorial framing, so what the file preserves is the argument as the room heard it.
