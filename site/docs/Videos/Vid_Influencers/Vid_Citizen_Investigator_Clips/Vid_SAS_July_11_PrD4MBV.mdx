## What This Video Is

Thirty-two minutes twenty-seven seconds of stage video from the Turning Point USA Student Action Summit in Tampa, capturing the full Charlie Kirk and Megyn Kelly conversation on 11 July 2025. It is a third-party capture carrying an APT watermark and a subscribe end card, not the official Turning Point feed.

This is the parent recording of the short Epstein and intelligence-services excerpt also filed in this section. Anyone who has seen only the two-minute clip is seeing about six per cent of the conversation, and the surrounding thirty minutes change how it reads.

## What Is Said

Kirk opens by asking the room to signal how much the subject matters: "make some noise if you care about the Jeffrey Epstein scandal. Raise your hand if it matters a lot to you." He reports the result himself: "every hand of 7,000 people."

Kelly's argument is about process and communications rather than about what is in any file. She locates the problem in how a two-page memorandum was released: given to a single outlet on a Sunday night at the end of a holiday weekend, unsigned. Her objection to the choice of outlet is blunt: "Our people don't read Axios."

Her prescription is the standard remedy for a story an institution wants closed: "Refuse to come out and give a presser? Come out and give a press conference, stand there for 6 hours to the point where people are dying of boredom. They've asked all their questions. That's how you put a scandal to rest."

She is critical of the Attorney General in strong terms and does so on the record, arguing that the earlier television appearances and the later memorandum cannot both be accurate. Those are Kelly's characterisations of a living official, offered as her reading of the public record; no response from Pam Bondi appears in the clip, and this site does not adopt the characterisation.

She contrasts that with the FBI leadership, noting that both men had discussed Epstein publicly before taking office and then went quiet, and that their one joint appearance was consistent with what they had said since. She reports, sourced only to herself, that one of them may resign over the handling, and Kirk notes the audience response when asked to choose between them.

Kirk repeatedly tries to keep the summit from becoming a single-subject event: "I don't want to talk about this as long as we have... the fact that young people can't afford homes is something we should talk about a lot more." Kelly makes the case back to him on transparency grounds: "One of the main reasons that we voted Trump in was because we wanted real answers on real stories, and we didn't want to be BS anymore."

The most-quoted stretch begins around the twenty-four minute mark, and it is the passage the short clip was cut from. Kirk asks: "Do you think there's any Intel community involvement in this?" Kelly answers "I wouldn't be surprised one bit," says her own reporting convinces her "he wasn't one of ours," and narrows the field to three foreign services. Asked which she would pick, she names one, citing an association. Both of them then immediately disclaim any evidentiary basis. Kelly: "It could be. I really don't know. And I have no evidence to suggest that." Kirk: "No, me neither."

Kirk then puts it to the room and reports the count aloud: "it's about half the hands think that it's being buried because of an Israeli thing." He goes on to reference the well-known reported account attributed to former United States attorney Alexander Acosta about being told to treat the case carefully, and Kelly draws a practical consequence: that the original search warrant was "very, very narrowly tailored," which is what she says the Bureau is now stuck with. She offers the deflating possibility as readily as the dramatic one: that the files may simply be disappointing and that current officials may not be to blame.

Kelly's proposal is disclosure without conditions: "I think every file should be released to the public the same way as the JFK files. Let the American people decide."

The conversation ends on advice to students, and Kelly's closing is nearly word for word what she would repeat on stage at Virginia Tech after Kirk's death, in another clip in this section: get the bad grade rather than write what you do not believe, and "wear it like a badge of honor."

## What The Footage Shows

Two people in white armchairs on a large lit stage, a small pedestal table with water bottles between them, microphones in hand. The camera work is static and repetitive: a medium two-shot against the pink Turning Point backdrop, a profile of Kelly against a dark starfield, a close-up of Kirk, and occasional wide shots of the auditorium. There is one genuinely evidentiary visual, at about thirty-six seconds into the excerpted section: an extreme wide shot of the room with the audience raising hands, which is the only independent check on Kirk's spoken count.

The shot list ends and the outro card appears well before the file's stated thirty-two minute runtime, which suggests the capture includes trailing material after the conversation closes.
