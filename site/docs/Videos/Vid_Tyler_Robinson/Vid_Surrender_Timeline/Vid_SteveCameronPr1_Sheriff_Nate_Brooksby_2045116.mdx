## What Is Said

Brooksby tells it as a personal story, and the human detail is part of why the clip circulates. He describes an ordinary call from an old friend, a retired colleague with whom occasional calls were not unusual:

"At 8:02 p.m. he calls me. Hey, I can tell his voice is kind of shaky, so my first thought is, who died? Like, who's he gonna tell me that died? And then I couldn't fathom what actually came out of his mouth."

He then reports what the caller said: "Hey, I know who Charlie Kirk's shooter is. I know the family through religious association, and he's in Washington County now, and we're working on trying to get him to come in voluntarily."

The remainder concerns the state the young man was said to be in and how the surrender was arranged: "During that phone call, some information was provided about Tyler potentially having some suicidal ideations, was en route to a remote area of Washington County. The parents convinced them not to do that, and conveyed that they would stand by them and help them surrender peacefully."

Three details in that minute have done a great deal of work in the investigation. The first is the time, 8:02 p.m., which the sheriff states unprompted and precisely. The second is that the chain to law enforcement ran through a private intermediary who knew the family, not through an investigative lead. The third is that the family are described as having actively steered the outcome toward a peaceful surrender.

This is a sheriff's public account given at a press conference. It is not sworn testimony, and a separate government document quoted elsewhere on this site records a call to a neighbouring sheriff at 2004 hours and an arrival at the Washington County Sheriff's Office at 2226 hours, which researchers have set against this narrative. The clip records what was said; whether the stated sequence holds is argued at the pages below.
