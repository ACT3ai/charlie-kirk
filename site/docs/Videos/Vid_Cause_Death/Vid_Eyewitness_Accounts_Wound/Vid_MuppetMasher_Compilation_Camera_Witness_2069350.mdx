## What Is Said

**The opening fragment (0:00 to about 0:14).** A female witness, unnamed in this
cut, repeats the same phrase three times over: "It was a direct shot to the
heart. It looked like it was a direct shot to the heart. It looked like, right
where his heart is, it started going red. He fell back, and went to his side."
She adds that she then looked at the crowd and "it all went to their knees,
really fast." This is the same account carried separately in this section from
The National Desk.

**The main interview.** The speaker gives her name on camera as
[Angie Martin](/People/angie-martin) and spells it for the interviewer. She
identifies herself as a retired police officer with twenty years of service —
four and a half years in the jail, four and a half in the courts, ten on the
road, and roles including K-9 officer, detective and school resource officer.
She says she taught active-shooter response.

*Where she was.* "I was about ready to go in at the front doors when I heard the
gunshot. So I tucked away under, 'cause I'm a retired police officer." She was
at the gate, not inside the crowd, and says she did not see the shot itself: "I
didn't actually observe the shot."

*What she did.* Her account is of crowd control rather than of the shooting. She
says her first concern was people being trampled as students rushed out, that
she grabbed two students, one close to fainting, and moved them through a tunnel
to shelter.

*On the direction of the report.* Asked whether she could tell where the gunshot
came from: "Yeah, I just looked up. It sound like it was above, but with
gunshots, it could echo." She volunteers the echo caveat herself, which is
unusual and worth crediting.

*On the man taken into custody.* She says she recognised him from a
Turning Point USA trip the previous year, names him as George Zinn, and then
immediately limits the claim: "I don't know if he's the shooter, but that is the
one they arrested." Later she repeats it: "I'm not saying he's a shooter, but I
recognized him as being the guy that was arrested." She offers some personal
impressions of him from past events. Those impressions are one person's
recollection about a living man, they are not evidence of anything, and they are
not repeated here. The public record is that authorities did not charge
[George Zinn](/distraction_people/George_Zinn) in connection with the shooting.

*The part that carries the most weight — her read of the wound.* Asked for her
general thoughts, she reasons from the wound description she had been given:

> Based on my experience with [shooters], you know, the way I was described where
> it hit his heart, and then blasted out this way, it would seem that it would
> have been shot from below, with it shot out this way. I didn't actually
> observe the shot. So it would be pretty hard to get a direct shot to the
> heart, you'd have to be a pretty good [marksman] to do that from that distance.

Two claims sit inside that. The first is geometric: a front-to-back path that
exits "this way" implies, to her, an upward angle from below rather than a level
shot from a rooftop. The second is about difficulty: she doubts a direct heart
hit at that range from an untrained shooter, and says of the man arrested, "I'm
not sure if George was that qualified or not." This is a twenty-year officer
reasoning aloud on a sidewalk from a second-hand wound description, not a
forensic determination, and she flags the limitation herself. It is recorded
here because it is one of the earliest on-camera statements from a person with
firearms training that the wound pattern did not read as a straight shot.

*On the conflicting witnesses she personally gathered.* This is the most
concrete contribution in the clip:

> I have two witnesses ... that were actually on the fourth row back ... [one
> said] it hit them in the neck. The girl I interviewed said it was a direct hit
> to the heart, and she saw it. So I called the police, let them know, got their
> names, so that we have the witnesses, and kept them here until ... he said we
> could go.

She is describing, on the day, the same chest-versus-neck split that later became
a research question in its own right — and she says she preserved both accounts
and passed the names to police.

*On the scene.* She says the heavy multi-agency and SWAT response was normal for
a campus incident, that the courtyard would be taped as a large crime scene, and
that with four to six thousand people present the processing would take a long
time. She closes: "Charlie Kirk's a hero, and I'm praying for his family."

At the time of the interview she says the last she had heard was that Kirk was
at the hospital in critical condition.
