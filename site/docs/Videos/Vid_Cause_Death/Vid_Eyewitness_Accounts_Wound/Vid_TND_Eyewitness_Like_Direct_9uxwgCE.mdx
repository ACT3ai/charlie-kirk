## What Is Said

One witness, speaking to the outlet:

> It was like a direct shot to the heart. It looked like right where his heart
> is, it started going red. He fell back and went to his side.

Read carefully, the statement is more cautious than the headline framing it
produced. The witness twice hedges — "like a direct shot," "it looked like" —
and the observation underneath the interpretation is about colour and location:
red spreading at the chest. The conclusion "to the heart" is the witness's own
inference from that, drawn in real time.

The last clause is a movement description that can be checked against footage:
"He fell back and went to his side."

Why this clip matters to the investigation is not whether this witness was
right. It is that this phrasing — heart, chest, direct — appears in several
independent on-camera interviews recorded within roughly the same window on
September 10, and then largely disappears from broadcast language once raw
bystander video spread and a neck injury became the dominant description. The
site treats the recurrence as an open pattern question, not as evidence of
coordination. Ordinary explanations have to be weighed first: shock distorts
recall, "heart" is common lay shorthand for a fatal upper-body hit, and
producers select for the most vivid soundbite available.

This is a witness account, recorded as given. It is not a medical finding, and
nothing here accuses this witness or the outlet of anything.
