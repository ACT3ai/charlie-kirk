## What This Video Is

A 12 minute 51 second reaction video posted by **Project Constitution** under
the headline "ALERT: This Is The MOST INSANE Charlie Kirk ASSASSINATION STORY
YOU'[VE HEARD]".

Its structure is worth stating precisely, because it changes what the clip is
evidence of. The host plays a long excerpt from **someone else's podcast** - an
interview with a young man who says he witnessed the shooting at Utah Valley
University - and then talks over and around it. The host is **not endorsing the
story**. He is picking it apart. About half the runtime is the embedded
interview and half is the host arguing that the account does not hold together.

So this page has two subjects: the witness's account as he tells it, and the
host's critique of it. Both are reported as claims. Neither is adopted.

## What Is Said

**The account, as the witness gives it.** He says a therapist publicly advertised
free initial consultations for anyone who had been at the Charlie Kirk event, and
that a friend sent him the details. He says he verified the practitioner was with
"a real agency", and he books an hour of EMDR - a trauma therapy - about six days
after September 10.

He describes the session going wrong by degrees. Fifteen or twenty minutes in,
he says, the practitioner is still talking at him rather than asking questions:
"I have been in therapy before... therapists usually ask you questions about
yourself, about the experience, and this was not happening." He says the session
was extended to two hours, that the practitioner moved onto his own politics and
theories, and that no EMDR was ever performed.

The turn, on his account, comes when he is asked what has helped him cope and
answers that he had given interviews to major news networks:

> "He then told me that because I was on these major news networks that I was the
> next target, that I was in mortal danger. He started pacing around the room."

He says he asked to leave, was told to wait for a second opinion, heard the door
"beep and latch", and that the practitioner returned after twenty minutes and
said: "What's new is that I'm going to have to keep you here... I cannot allow
you to leave knowing the circumstance that you were in."

He states he was told he would be physically detained and that police would be
called, that he had earlier been told the practitioner was carrying a firearm,
and that he texted a family member during it. He says the situation ended when he
demanded to speak to a supervisor, and the supervisor told the practitioner
plainly: "Even if you were a danger to yourself or others, we are not legally
allowed to hold you here... you need to let him go."

Nobody in the clip names the practitioner, the agency, or the supervisor. This
page names nobody either, and it records the account as **an allegation made by
one person in a podcast interview**. It is not established that any of it
occurred, and no finding of wrongdoing is made against anyone.

**The host's critique.** This is the second half of the video and it is
substantially sceptical. His objections, in his own framing:

> "He said he won't name the agency, and he also won't name the therapist, which
> is weird - because why would you share a story about a therapist... holding you
> hostage against your will, and you're not going to expose who this person is so
> it can[not] happen [to] other people?"

He objects to the tone: "you start talking about this in a laughing manner. Then
you try to make it serious." He objects to the absence of any consequence: "No
police report, but you was kidnapped... nobody went to jail for this. Or
nothing. And now other people are at risk."

And he states the general instinct behind the whole segment:

> "Have you ever listened to a story and you say, all right, bro, it's something
> you're not sharing?"

He closes by wondering aloud whether the function of the story, told publicly,
was to establish that the speaker was present at the event at all - noting that
few of the widely quoted early witnesses were UVU students.

## What The Footage Shows

Talking heads and screen capture. The host in his own frame; the embedded podcast
interview playing as a window; no documents, no recordings from the session, no
correspondence, and no images of any location described. There is nothing visual
in this clip that corroborates any part of the account.

That is the central point for anyone weighing it. The story is **entirely
testimonial and entirely unverified within the video**, and the video's own host
says so at length. What would move it out of that category is specific and
absent: a named agency, a complaint filed with a state licensing board, a police
report, the supervisor's account, or the text messages the witness says he sent
during the session.

Two things nonetheless make this clip worth keeping. First, the described
scenario - a witness being told, days afterward, by someone in a position of
authority, that speaking publicly had made him a target - is the pattern the site
tracks at [Therapy Intimidation](/CoverUp/Therapy_Intimidation), and this is one
of the primary artefacts behind that page. Second, the video is an unusual
example of a channel in this space **arguing against** a story that would suit
its priors. That is worth noting on its own terms.

The honest summary: one unverified first-person allegation, no named parties, no
documentary support, and a host who spends half the runtime saying he does not
believe it as told.
