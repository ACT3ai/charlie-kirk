## What This Video Is

Eleven minutes sixteen seconds, and it is the most important single file in this archive.

This is the Blake Bednarz UVU original - the unaltered camera capture, not a repost, not a re-encode, and not a social-media copy. It is held as roughly three gigabytes of source footage and it is published to IPFS so that it cannot be taken down. It travels with two companion documents kept alongside it in this repository: an original metadata report and a video forensic information sheet.

That pairing is what makes it a reference original rather than just another angle. Almost every other clip of this event in circulation - including several dozen in this archive - has been through at least one platform re-encode, and many have been cropped, upscaled, captioned, watermarked, slowed, or had their audio replaced. Comparisons made between two such copies are comparisons between two sets of compression artefacts. A file with intact metadata and a documented chain is what anyone attempting a serious frame-level analysis has to work from.

It is the same session captured in the eleven-minute wide angle held elsewhere in this archive, and the two transcripts overlap almost exactly. The difference is not what is recorded but what condition it is in.

## What Is Said

The audio is the last eleven minutes of the question-and-answer session, recorded from the audience.

The long middle is a theological exchange between Charlie Kirk and a student about evidence for the Latter-day Saint tradition versus mainstream Christianity. Kirk repeatedly heads off the framing that he is attacking the church - "the audience gets the impression I'm against LDS, which I'm really not" - and closes the thread by telling the student to "show me the core evidence of why you believe what you believe, specifically archaeologically... I think that's an important thing you need to pray about." When the student presses the symmetry argument, Kirk answers with a list: the empty tomb, "the over 500 witnesses," the Apostle Paul, the Gospel of Luke, the female witnesses - "if you're gonna make up the resurrection, you wouldn't use female witnesses" - and external sources, "Josephus and the Talmud."

The final exchange is the one the investigation returns to. A questioner takes the microphone, refers to a recent mass shooting and to a policy proposal that followed it, and asks:

> Do you know how many transgender Americans have been mass shooters over the last 10 years?

Kirk answers "too many," then "five is a lot, right? I'm gonna give you some credit." The questioner asks how many mass shooters there have been in America over the same decade, and adds the qualifier:

> Counting or not counting gang violence.

Kirk's one-word reply - "Great" - is the last thing on the recording before the shot. That clause is the synchronisation marker for the entire corpus. Every angle that contains it can be aligned to this file, and because this file is the least-degraded copy, it is the one the alignment should be measured against.

## What The Footage Shows

A sustained view of the tent, the table, the speaker and the crowd from the audience, running unbroken through the last eleven minutes of the session and terminating seconds after the shot. Visible throughout: the canopy branding, the sponsor banner behind the table, the water bottles and papers on the table, the crowd-control barricades, and the professional cameras on tripods at the stage edge.

What makes it forensically useful is not what it frames but what has not been done to it. Frame timing is intact. Audio and video are in their original relationship. The metadata report records the capture device and the file's properties. Any claim about this event that depends on a single frame - a flash, a movement, a timing gap of a fraction of a second - should be tested against this file and not against a re-encoded copy of it, because at that level of detail a platform re-encode is capable of manufacturing the very artefact being argued about.

Because the media is large and pinned to IPFS rather than committed to the repository, the file is retrieved by its content identifier. The commands to pull and pin it, along with the two companion documents, are kept with the archive's IPFS index.

This footage records a man's death. It is preserved and published because the alternative - a case argued entirely from degraded copies of degraded copies - serves nobody.
