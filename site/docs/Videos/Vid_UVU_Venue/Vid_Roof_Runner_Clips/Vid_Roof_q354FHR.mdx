## What This Video Is

One hour, nine minutes and forty-three seconds of continuous first-person livestream, recorded by an independent streamer walking the Utah Valley University campus on the day of the event. It is by far the longest single artifact in this section, and unlike the rest of it, it is not a fragment: the recording runs from well before the event through the shot itself and on into the evacuation, without a cut.

The streamer is an amateur working with self-built kit, and he says so on camera — an early conversation with another camera operator is entirely about his rig, a small micro-four-thirds camera, a wireless microphone and receiver, a router in his backpack for cellular uplink, and an SD card recording locally while the stream goes out. That detail matters for evidentiary purposes: a local recording alongside a live broadcast means a copy survives even where the stream drops, and at one point somebody tells him his stream had in fact cut out an hour earlier.

The file sits in the investigation's capture archive as `Roof.webm` with no posting account attached. It has not been published to IPFS, so a still frame stands in for the player.

## What Is Said

This is a talking video, and the speech is the evidence. Roughly three phases.

**Before.** The streamer works the crowd, and most of what he records is ordinary campus politics. He walks past chanting supporters, notes "look at all the red caps," and then deliberately crosses to what he calls the protest side to interview counter-demonstrators. Those interviews run several minutes and are, by the standards of the day, notably civil: students explain placards reading "DON'T LEGALIZE RACISM" and "FREE PALESTINE," argue with him about when life begins, and are pressed on their positions. One says of the Palestinian cause, "We also don't condone Hamas. We think that genocides are wrong on both sides." Another says the sign's second line, "love one another," is a reference to the teachings of Jesus. Chants hostile to Kirk by name, some of them obscene, are audible in the background at several points. All of these speakers are unnamed students and none is identified here.

**The moment.** The shot arrives mid-interview. The transcript records the streamer's own reaction — profanity, then "There's been a shooting. Heard shots... We heard shots" — followed almost immediately by uncertainty: "I don't know if that was shots or if that was fireworks, to be honest." Some minutes later he corrects himself flatly: "Definitely not fireworks."

**After.** From that point the recording becomes something rarer than any of the phone clips in this section: a reporter with a microphone, on the ground, collecting eyewitness statements within minutes of the event, before anyone has heard an official account to align with.

The first information reaches him second-hand and he labels it as such: "Charlie Kirk got shot in the neck, is what my kid is saying... This is unconfirmed. This is from my son who was down there, right in front of Charlie Kirk."

A man who gives his first name as Eric describes what he saw from close range:

> I was standing about the 3rd level back. So probably 50 feet from Charlie. I heard a shot, which took my attention away from Charlie. And when I look back at him after the echoes, there was blood protruding from his neck or his head about 6 inches or more, literally gushing out. Neck or head, I'm not quite sure, because his head was tilted back.

Asked whether it sounded like a close shooter or a long shot, Eric says he does not know, and then describes the sound itself: "It sounded like a higher caliber, like a 9 millimeter. It didn't sound like a 45 or anything... It didn't sound that deep to me. It almost sounded like a cherry bomb."

A second witness, interviewed later in the stream, gives a different vantage and a consistent injury description:

> I was standing from probably the 3rd row in the bottom. I saw the shot ring out. I looked to my left, and then I looked over to my right. I saw him slump over in his chair forward. He slumped over. He had blood coming from the left side of his neck, down his shirt, and he slumped over and he was carried off.

A third places the sound directionally: "It came out of my right. I was right in the center, on the top level, and it came out the right side."

Two other threads run through the after-phase and are worth isolating.

The first is the roof. A bystander tells the streamer that "one of the cops said there might be a guy on the roof," that officers then re-entered and evacuated a building, and adds, "I saw a cop on that roof right over there." The streamer, watching police work a building, speculates aloud on his own stream: "It's looking like this might have been a sniper outpost up here on this building, possibly. Because that would be the only spot to reach down to the courtyard." That is a livestreamer's guess in the first minutes of a chaotic scene, not an analysis, and it should be read as such. He also relays, hedged, that "the police said it could have come from up on the roof, but they arrested a guy down right in front of Charlie" — an early, on-the-ground version of the confusion between the roof and the man detained in the crowd.

The second is screening. A ticketed attendee tells him, on camera and angrily:

> Well, there was no security. I mean, I got tickets, and they didn't even take the tickets to get us in. They just let everybody in there. So there was no police, no security... Walked in with tripods sticking out of my backpack, no problem. Right through the door.

That is one attendee's account of his own entry, recorded within an hour of the event and before any official statement about screening existed. It is first-hand and contemporaneous, which is its value; it is also a single person's experience at a single door, which is its limit.

Also captured: a photographer nearby tells the streamer he has photographed the man who was detained and is taking the image to UVU police; and two students, unprompted, remark on the timing — "what a coincidence that he was talking about gun violence, and then all of a sudden this happens."

## What The Footage Shows

Visually the recording is ordinary, and that is the point. A handheld first-person walk: crowded elevated walkways, a packed lower plaza, white and blue canopy tents including one branded for the tour, an interior hallway hung with green UVU banners, parking lots, the Student Life and Wellness Center, mountains under a clear sky. Campus security officers appear in the crowd at several points. Nothing in the frames shows the stage at the moment of the shot, no weapon is visible, and no figure on a roof is visible in this recording — despite the filename. The claim about a rooftop position is made in speech, by bystanders and by the streamer, and is not corroborated by anything this camera captured.
