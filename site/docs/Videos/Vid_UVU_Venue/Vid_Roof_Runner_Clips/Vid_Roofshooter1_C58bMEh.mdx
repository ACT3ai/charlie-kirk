The second copy of the same audio renders the opening line as "You think he'd already be in place?" — a small difference between speech engines, and a reminder that neither rendering is authoritative down to the word.

Three things in that exchange are worth separating out.

The first is the observation itself: one speaker states that there is a person on a roof, and points. That is a direct, contemporaneous sighting by someone standing on the ground, and it is the reason the clip exists.

The second is the movement claim: "He just ran from over there, ran in." The speaker is describing motion he says he watched — a figure crossing from one area and going in — and he places himself at the scene: "And I was right there."

The third is the reasoning, and it is the part that is easiest to over-weight. "You think you'd already be in place? You would think that" is two bystanders doing exactly what everyone did that afternoon, out loud: testing whether what they are seeing fits an assassination. It is inference by people with no more information than their own eyes. The repeated "just saying" is the speaker flagging his own uncertainty. Reported as what it is — an on-the-spot eyewitness impression — it is genuinely useful. Read as a conclusion about who was on that roof or why, it is not evidence at all, and the speakers do not claim it is.

Neither man is named in the file, and neither is named here.
