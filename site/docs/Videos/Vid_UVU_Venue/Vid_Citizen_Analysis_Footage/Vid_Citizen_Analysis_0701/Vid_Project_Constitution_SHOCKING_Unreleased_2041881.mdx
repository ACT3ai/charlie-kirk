## What This Video Is

A 1 minute 3 second vertical phone capture, circulated by Project Constitution
under a headline calling it unreleased footage. It is raw bystander video, not an
edit: one continuous handheld take from the elevated concrete walkway above the
plaza, with no cuts, no captions and no music. A small circular watermark sits in
the top right corner.

Footage of this kind is more useful than most of what surrounds it, precisely
because nothing has been done to it. The camera operator is not making an
argument. He is reacting, badly and in real time, and the record of that is the
value.

## What Is Said

The audio is chaotic and mostly not addressed to anyone. The operator's
first reaction lands around four seconds in as a shocked expletive, twice. At
about eight seconds he is shouting instructions at people near him on the
walkway -- "get up, get up, get up" -- which is worth noting because it is the
opposite of the reflex the phrase implies: people around him had gone to the
ground.

The remainder is fragmentary exchange between him and at least one other man
nearby, working out what happened while it is still happening. The words that
carry any content are the ones where they try to name it. One asks whether it was
a gun. The other answers, "no, I'm not... I hope not," and the reply comes back,
"yeah, I hope not." Near the end one of them says, "no, bro, he went like..." and
breaks off, and then twice, "I swear to God."

Nobody in this clip claims to have seen a shooter, a weapon or a direction. The
transcription on file is partial and garbles the opening line, which is expected
given the noise floor; the useful content is the tone and the timing rather than
the wording.

## What The Footage Shows

The frame opens looking down from the walkway across the plaza, with a dense
crowd on the grassed terrace below, white event tents and banners beyond, a
two-storey glass-fronted building running along the left, and clear midday sun
throwing hard shadows.

Then the crowd moves. A ripple runs through the seated audience and people start
running up onto and past the camera. The operator pans hard left and right,
catching a man in a grey hoodie crossing the walkway, three women running toward
the lens holding on to each other, and a young man in a white shirt sprinting
past on the right. Through the middle third, people stream toward the camera,
many glancing back over their shoulders as they come.

From about 0:23 the character of the footage changes and that change is the
substantive part. The rush thins. Individual people slow to a walk. One man in a
black shirt walks calmly the other way, against the flow. Others stop at the
walkway edge and stand looking back down at the plaza, trying to see what
happened. By the final ten seconds the panic has drained out of the frame and
what is left is a crowd standing around, talking, staring.

What the clip therefore documents is the shape and duration of the crowd
reaction on that specific side of the plaza -- how fast it started, how far it
propagated, how quickly it decayed. What it does not document is the event
itself. The camera is pointed away from the stage the whole time. There is no
shot visible in it, no stage, no shooter, no rooftop. The headline's promise of
unreleased assassination footage is not what the frames deliver.
