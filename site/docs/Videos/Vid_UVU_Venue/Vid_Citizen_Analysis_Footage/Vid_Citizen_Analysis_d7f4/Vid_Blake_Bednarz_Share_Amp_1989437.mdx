## What This Video Is

Thirty minutes fifteen seconds, posted to X by Blake Bednarz with a request to share and bookmark it. His stated purpose is in the caption: two videos combined into one continuous record, so that the sequence can be watched without cuts. It is the longest single artefact in this section and the most useful, because it is assembled for timeline reconstruction rather than for argument.

The material is raw: fixed campus security angles, handheld phone footage, and a vlogger's livestream that keeps running through the evacuation and into a string of impromptu interviews. Closing cards state that the UVU Review video ends at 12:24:29 p.m. and that the livestream by the second recordist ran one hour nine minutes forty-two seconds, and sign off with a justice hashtag.

## What Is Said

The first ten minutes are the event itself. The clip catches the exchange that was under way when the recording is interrupted, an audience member pressing Kirk on gun policy after a shooting:

"A few weeks ago there was a transgender mass shooter... do you know how many transgender Americans have been mass shooters over the last 10 years?"

Kirk answers "Too many," and then puts the question back: "Do you know how many mass shooters there have been in America over the last 10 years? Counting or not counting gang violence." That is where the recording breaks.

What follows is the most valuable part of the file, because it is uncoached and contemporaneous. Voices overlap: "There's been a shooting. We heard shots. You guys okay?" A recordist begins narrating live: "We are here live at Utah Valley State University. There's been a shooting at the Charlie Kirk event." He immediately flags his own uncertainty about what he heard: "I don't know if that was shots or if that was fireworks, to be honest."

The neck-wound account enters the record within seconds, and it enters as hearsay, which the recordist says out loud: "My son just said Charlie Kirk was shot in the neck. I don't know the accuracy of that. This is unconfirmed."

Later he attributes the same thing to staff: "Charlie Kirk got hit is what the security detail just said."

The interviews that follow are the section researchers return to. A man who gives his name as Eric describes his own vantage: "I was standing about the third level back, so probably 50 feet from Charlie. I heard a shot, which took my attention away from Charlie. And when I looked back at him after the echoes, there was blood protruding from his neck or his head about six inches or more, literally gushing out." Asked whether the shooter was close or distant, he will not guess: "I don't know. It sounded like a higher caliber... It almost sounded like a cherry bomb."

Another attendee describes the same thing from the third row: "I heard the shot ring out. I looked to my left and then I looked over to my right. I saw him slump over in his chair forward. He had blood coming from the left side of his neck, down his shirt."

Two other threads surface in the crowd audio. Several people voice suspicion about the timing on the spot, one saying the event "was set up because the second speaker was the one talking about guns," and another marvelling at "what a coincidence that he was talking about gun violence and then all of a sudden this happens." And the origin of the shot is unsettled in real time: "The police said it could have come from up on the roof, but they arrested a guy up down right in front of Charlie."

A photographer is described as having captured the person taken into custody in the courtyard and as heading to campus police with the image.

These are bystander impressions recorded in the first minutes, not findings. They are quoted here because their value is precisely that they predate any official account.

## What The Footage Shows

Roughly the first two minutes are a high fixed security angle over the "Prove Me Wrong" tent with the crowd gathered around the stage. The compilation then alternates between that feed, a closer handheld shot of the stage, and further security angles across the plaza. The disruption itself lands at about ten minutes sixteen seconds, marked on screen with the text "Sound 12:23:29pm."

From there the record is evacuation: wide high angles of the courtyard emptying, people climbing the terraced grass amphitheatre and the concrete stairs, then eye-level handheld footage following the crowd into the Pope Science building and through its corridors and atrium. The last stretch is outdoors, holding on a bearded man in a plaid shirt and grey cap discussing what he saw, before the closing text cards.

The on-screen timecodes are the reason to keep this file: 12:13 p.m., 12:23:29 p.m. for the sound, and 12:24:29 p.m. for the end of the UVU Review video. Those give an independent anchor for anyone reconciling recordings that have been re-encoded or trimmed.
