## What This Video Is

Eleven minutes and fifteen seconds, shot from an elevated position - a balcony or upper walkway - looking down on the tent and the crowd. It is the longest continuous stage-facing angle in this section and it runs right through the event, ending seconds after the shot.

It is a fixed-position capture with occasional zooms and one pan; the operator is not identified and there is no caption or watermark. What makes it valuable is not image quality but duration. Almost every other citizen angle of this event is a fragment of a few seconds. This one gives the eleven minutes of context that the fragments were cut out of.

## What Is Said

The audio is a full question-and-answer session, machine-transcribed from a distance, so individual words are unreliable while the shape of the exchange is clear.

The long middle section is a theological debate between Charlie Kirk and a student over evidence for the Latter-day Saint tradition versus mainstream Christianity. Kirk opens by heading off the framing: "First of all, I love Mormons, so I've said that... this is just a fun polite discussion." He returns to that several times, including near the end - "the audience gets the impression I'm against LDS. Generally not" - and closes the thread by telling the questioner to "show me the core evidence of why you believe what you believe, specifically archaeologically... that's an important thing you need to pray about."

The student presses the symmetry argument, that neither tradition has independent evidence. Kirk answers with a list: the empty tomb, "the over 500 witnesses," the Apostle Paul, the Gospel of Luke, the female witnesses ("if you're gonna make up the resurrection, you wouldn't use female witnesses"), and external sources - "Josephus and the Talmud." The exchange stays civil and is, in tone, an ordinary campus debate.

The last exchange is the one that matters to the investigation. A new questioner takes the microphone. The transcript places him as follows:

> A few weeks ago, there was a transgender mass shooter, and ever since then, about three weeks later, the Trump DOJ said that we should start talking about revoking gun rights for transgender Americans... do you know how many transgender Americans have been mass shooters over the last 10 years?

Kirk answers "too many," and "five is a lot, right?" The questioner then asks how many mass shooters there have been in America over the same ten years, and adds the qualifier that ends the clip:

> Counting or not counting gang violence.

Kirk's reply - "Great" - is the last word audible before the shot. That clause is the synchronisation marker shared by every angle in this section.

The questioner is identified elsewhere on this site; the exchange itself is a policy question about statistics, and nothing on this page should be read as attributing anything beyond it to him.
