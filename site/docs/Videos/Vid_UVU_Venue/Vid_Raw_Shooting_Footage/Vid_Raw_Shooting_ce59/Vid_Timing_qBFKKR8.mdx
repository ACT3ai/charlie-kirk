## What This Video Is

A one-minute-fifty-five edit that synchronises two independent camera sources from September 10, 2025 into a single timeline. It is not raw footage. Someone assembled it, and the assembly is the point: for the first 36 seconds the frame is split, with hand-held bystander video of the stage on the left and a static campus security camera feed on the right, running at the same time. At 0:36 the left panel goes black and the security feed takes the full frame for the remaining eighty seconds.

The filename is "Timing," and that is exactly what the edit is for. It exists to let a viewer watch the moment on stage and the movement outside the building against the same clock. Whoever built it is not identified on the video, and no watermark or credit appears.

One correction to the automated description attached to this file: it places the venue in South Jordan, Utah. The stage banners, the plaza, the building and the parking lot are Utah Valley University in Orem. The file is correctly clustered; the location guess in its metadata is wrong.

## What Is Said

Very little, and all of it in the first fifteen seconds. The recovered audio runs:

> "Let me give you give you credit. Do you know how many mass shooters there have been in America over the last 10 years? Counting or not counting gang violence? Great. Go, go, go, go! Get out of. Everybody follow the left! Go, go, go, go... Stay down."

Two voices. The first is the Q&A exchange at the "Prove Me Wrong" table — an audience member putting a statistical question about mass shootings, and the speaker on stage answering. The clause "Counting or not counting gang violence?" is the line widely reported as Charlie Kirk's last words; the machine transcript here recovers it in sequence immediately before the bang, which is consistent with that reporting. The transcript does not cleanly separate questioner from answerer, so the exact division of the lines should be taken from a cleaner source rather than from this file.

The second voice set is security personnel, from roughly 0:15: "Go, go, go, go! Get out of..." and "Everybody follow the left!" — evacuation commands, shouted, overlapping. A final "Stay down" lands near the end of the hand-held panel. The strong language in the crowd audio is bystander reaction and adds nothing.

There is no narration anywhere on this edit. Nobody explains what you are watching.

## What The Footage Shows

**Left panel, 0:00 to 0:36 — the stage.** A hand-held camera pans across the crowd in front of the "Prove Me Wrong" banners, then closes on the seated speaker at the table as he takes the question. At about 0:08 a sharp report sounds and the speaker drops behind the table instantly. The camera falls and shakes. At 0:10 to 0:15 it catches a security guard taking a man to the ground near the front row — an incident distinct from the shot itself and worth separating from it. From 0:15 the panel becomes a run of chaotic cuts: guards shouting, the principal being moved. At 0:26 the camera is inside an open-sided concrete parking structure; a dark vehicle pulls up, the principal is put into the back seat, and it drives off by about 0:36.

**Right panel, 0:08 onward — the security feed.** A static wide-angle camera looking across the institutional building, its lawns and a large asphalt parking lot. It shows the crowd reaction from a distance, behind glass. From 0:36 it takes the full frame. From roughly 0:40 a figure in dark clothing is visible moving away from the building across the grass. The camera pans and digitally zooms to keep the figure centred — it occupies about two percent of the frame — tracking it past a large tree, into the parking lot, then between rows of parked cars, until it exits frame near the main street at about 1:50. Cars begin to leave the lot behind it.

What the edit establishes and what it does not are different things, and the difference matters.

It establishes a synchronisation: a report on the stage audio, and a figure crossing open ground away from the building within seconds of it, on a fixed camera whose feed can be timestamped. That is a genuine contribution and it is why the clip is filed with the raw shooting footage.

It does not establish who the figure is. At two percent of frame, through a security camera's digital zoom, no identification is possible from these pixels, and nothing on the edit attempts one. It also does not establish that the two panels are truly frame-accurate to each other — that alignment is the editor's work, not a property of the source material, and no sync method is shown. A viewer should treat the pairing as a proposed reconstruction to be checked against the original feeds, not as a verified timestamp.
