## What This Video Is

Fifty seconds, vertical, shot on a phone from inside the crowd and posted to TikTok. The creator watermark on screen reads @caseypbryson. It carries one line of overlay text, and that line is the reason the clip matters: **"I turned the camera off right before the gun went off."**

So this is an attendee's ordinary recording of the event that stops, by the filmer's own account, seconds before the moment everyone has spent months trying to see. It is a near-miss piece of evidence, and it is honest about being one.

The setting is unambiguous. The white pop-up tent carries "THE AMERICAN COMEBACK TOUR" and "PROVE ME WRONG", the crowd wear campaign and BYU caps, and the terraces and mountains behind match the UVU courtyard.

One correction belongs on this page. The automated visual description generated for this file names the seated speaker under the tent as Steven Crowder. That identification is very likely wrong: the man in the white short-sleeved polo seated at the table with the microphone, under that branding, on that stage, is consistent with Charlie Kirk. Treat the name in the machine description as an error, not as a finding.

## What Is Said

There is no narration. The audio is crowd noise and a partly audible Q-and-A exchange from the tent microphone, which the machine transcript captures only in fragments: "Unless you tell me, sir…", a garbled name, "…and your discussion of religion", then "my question."

That is enough to establish what is happening — a member of the public at the microphone is framing a question, and the answer is in progress — but not enough to establish who is speaking or which question in the sequence this is. It should not be matched to any named questioner on the strength of this audio. The transcript is too degraded, and the surrounding words are lost.

## What The Footage Shows

The camera is handheld, unstable, and shooting over the heads of people in front.

From 0:00 to 0:06 it holds a close shot of the seated speaker under the tent, microphone in hand, gesturing and looking off-camera toward the questioner. From 0:06 to 0:19 it pans left across the crowd on the outdoor bleachers, then tilts up to show more spectators standing on the balconies of the building opposite, with the mountains behind. From 0:19 to 0:30 it returns right to the tent. From 0:30 to 0:35 it pans left again over the crowd and the surrounding architecture. From 0:35 to 0:41 it comes back right, catching production and security staff in black branded t-shirts working the barrier line near the stage.

Then, at 0:41, a hand in a red sleeve moves across the lens and the frame goes completely dark. The last four seconds are the TikTok end card.

What that leaves behind is genuinely useful even though it stops short. In fifty seconds it documents the balcony positions occupied by spectators, the sightlines from the bleachers to the tent, the barrier line and where staff were standing, and the density and distribution of the crowd — all in the final minutes, all from ground level, all before anything happened. Very little footage in this corpus covers the "before" at all; almost everything starts at the bang.

The overlay claim is also checkable in a way most claims here are not. If the filmer stopped recording immediately before the shot, then the file's own creation and modification timestamps put a hard bound on the moment, and the last frames are the closest visual record of the courtyard as it stood. That would require the original file rather than a re-uploaded copy, which is exactly what the site keeps asking for.

Nobody in the crowd should be identified from this footage. They are attendees who happen to be in frame.
