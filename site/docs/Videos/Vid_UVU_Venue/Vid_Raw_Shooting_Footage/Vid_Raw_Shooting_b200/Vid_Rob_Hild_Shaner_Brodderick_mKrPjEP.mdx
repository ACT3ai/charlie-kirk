## What This Video Is

The slides are the substance — an FBI Salt Lake City post seeking information on a person of interest, with two security-camera stills; an Instagram post by an account rendered on screen as @shaneintheusa; several photographs of four young men on a concrete staircase; and a team photograph in blue jerseys with one figure circled.

## What Is Said

Owens narrates the Instagram post first, reading from it: the account holder wrote that he "took my friends to my grandma's house tonight and we retraced his steps and we took pictures in the exact place that the security cameras caught him," and added that he was "still very glad to have attended and to support Charlie Kirk and to see him." She notes the post ends with a picture of Converse-brand footprints and a string of hashtags.

Her actual assertion is much narrower than the framing of the slides suggests, and it is worth quoting because of that. What she says she wants is identification, not attribution: "I'm looking at the front on the right and I'm like — I don't know. I'd like to know who that is at the very least. And I'd like to know who everybody in this photo is."

She then explains what struck her about the post itself rather than about any person in it: "This is just interesting — like, you guys are in the staircase and posting side by side. Like, they put these side by side photos of them in the staircase." Her stated reason for wanting names is that two of the four were not findable: "I need to know more about these two individuals, who I couldn't find in his friend's list, and they certainly hadn't liked the photo. I also would have messaged them directly to figure out who they are. And yeah, I found that very strange."

That is the whole of it. She asks who two people are and says the post struck her as odd. She does not say that anyone in the photographs was involved in anything, and this page does not either.

The slides do more than her words do. They place a private individual's photograph beside the person-of-interest stills and beside images of the charged defendant, with a red circle drawn on him. This site's position on that name is set out on its own page and is unambiguous: it appears only in unverified online speculation, there is no evidence connecting the individual to any wrongdoing, no charges of any kind have been reported, and "an ordinary, innocent explanation fully accounts for the mention." Nothing on this page should be read as qualifying that.

There is also a plain innocent reading of the underlying Instagram post, and it deserves stating because the video does not state it. Young people who attended a mass-casualty event and are processing it publicly do exactly this: they go back to the site, they photograph where things happened, they post about it, and they say they are glad they went. It reads as morbid to a stranger and as normal grief behaviour to anyone who has watched a campus react to something like this.

## What The Footage Shows

The slide sequence runs: the FBI Salt Lake City post at 0:00; a comparison slide of the charged defendant, the person-of-interest image and a circled private individual at 0:01; security footage beside a selfie at 0:03; the Instagram caption slide at 0:05; the team photograph at 0:07; then Owens on camera from 0:09; the Instagram post and staircase photographs from 0:17 to 0:50; a repeat of the comparison slide at 0:50; a final selfie slide with a white circle from 0:58; and Owens again to close.

Two things are worth separating out. The staircase photographs and the Instagram caption are real primary material — a public post by an identifiable account, quoted directly. The comparison slides are not primary material; they are an argument, assembled by whoever made the deck, using red and white circles to direct the eye. A viewer should treat those two categories differently, and the video does not signal the difference.

Nothing in any of the frames connects any person in the photographs to the shooting. The comparison rests on a resemblance between low-resolution security stills and social-media snapshots, which is the least reliable identification method there is and the one most consistently overturned in real cases.
