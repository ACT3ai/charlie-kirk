## What This Video Is

Eleven minutes fifty-two seconds. Two things are happening at once and they are unrelated to each other.

The picture is a first-person walkthrough of the Utah Valley University campus, filmed after the event: a concrete underpass, a metal staircase, an elevated plaza, the Losee Center and Woodbury and Sparks Automotive buildings, a large American flag hung from the pedestrian bridge, police tape and parked police vehicles, and a small flower memorial on the grass with a hand-written sign for Charlie. A man in a white t-shirt and a white "47" cap appears on camera at intervals to point at landmarks.

The audio is something else entirely: a recorded telephone conversation, played over the walkthrough. That call is the reason this page exists, and it is one of the more unusual documents in the whole video corpus, because it is a piece of online speculation being taken apart in real time by the two people on the line.

## What Is Said

The call is between the man filming — who describes himself as an independent researcher, and states plainly, "I don't talk to Candace Owens at all. I've never talked to her in my life. I'm just doing an independent investigation myself based on what she put out, because I want to verify or debunk it myself" — and a friend of the three students whose photographs had circulated after a podcast segment placed one of them beside the person-of-interest imagery.

The friend's opening is the strongest statement of the harm the speculation was doing. On the resemblance: "I know that he looks lanky and tall, like — again, the video — but out there, that's half of the state of Utah." On the reasoning: "Candace comes out and says, well, nobody's reaching out to me, so he's suspicious. Like, yeah, because he's a normal guy. He's not a public figure." And on the effect: "you're putting normal people in danger for no good reason."

The researcher does not argue. He states his own position — "I am trying to get to the bottom of the truth, whatever that may be" — concedes the resemblance is only a resemblance, and offers, unprompted, to do the opposite of what he was accused of doing: "if I can clear him, then that's great. Then I'll publicly state it and make sure everybody knows he's innocent."

The friend then gives the account. All three went to the event together and were in the crowd together, near the front. Afterwards, with the campus locked down and nobody knowing what was happening, they went to a house nearby belonging to a grandmother of one of them and stayed there for cover until the area cleared. He says he saw them there himself, roughly ten minutes from the venue, and that photographs exist of them at the house. The retracing-of-steps photographs that started all of this came later and were, on his account, exactly what they looked like: young men going back to look at where something happened.

The researcher asks the questions an investigator should ask rather than the ones an accuser would: where in the crowd, left or right of the tent, on the balcony or inside, and what each of them was wearing that day. The friend sends him a video from the event itself, and the researcher explains what he intends to do with it: pull the metadata, check the timestamp, and match the clothing. "You should see the timestamp. At 33, you can see him, and you can see the outfits." His closing commitment is explicit: "give me probably ten minutes or so to check the metadata, and then if it checks out, yeah, I'll put a post up saying that."

They also work through the one thing that had made the researcher suspicious in the first place, and it is a useful exchange because it is the general case. An Instagram account was deleted. The researcher's instinct was that "guilty people do stuff like that." The friend explains: "the people online, there's always someone that'll put your life in danger for nothing. When it's you and your family… you're gonna nuke it, even if it looks weird to some investigators on Twitter." The researcher accepts it: "You explained why he did it and that makes sense to me." And the friend adds the point that actually settles it — if any of them had been involved, "he wouldn't have posted these pictures in the first place."

The name of a Utah political figure comes up once as having also been at the event; the machine transcript garbles the surrounding words badly enough that nothing should be built on that passage.

This page reports the call for the reason the call is valuable: it is exculpatory. Three private individuals who have been accused of nothing were the subject of online resemblance speculation, an account of their movements was given, and the person checking it agreed on the record to publish a correction if it held up.
