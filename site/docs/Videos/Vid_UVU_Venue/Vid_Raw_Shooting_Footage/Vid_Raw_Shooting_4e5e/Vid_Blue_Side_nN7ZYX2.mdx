Set against the frames themselves, the claim runs into problems that are worth
stating so a reader does not have to take either side on faith:

* **The "object".** The zoom shows a small light-coloured shape near a hand and
  torso in a heavily compressed, digitally enlarged phone image. At that
  magnification a phone, a lanyard, a pair of sunglasses, a badge, a water
  bottle cap, or a fold of shirt all render as the same few pixels. The captions
  name it as an object and then narrate it being concealed; the pixels do not
  support any identification of what it is.
* **The hand signals.** Raising a hand toward the head and gesturing while
  standing in a crowd are among the most common movements a person makes.
  Matching a single frame of that against a chart of military signals is a
  method that will produce a hit on almost any crowd footage of sufficient
  length. The chart is stock material and is not connected to these men by
  anything except the edit.
* **The reaction argument.** "Does not react like everyone else" is offered as
  suspicious. Startle responses in crowds are famously heterogeneous - freezing,
  delayed reaction and continuing to look the wrong way are all ordinary. A
  single person not ducking is not, by itself, informative.
* **The trajectory line.** The red line is drawn by the editor. It is a graphic,
  not a measurement, and it is not derived from any ballistic reconstruction
  presented in the clip.
