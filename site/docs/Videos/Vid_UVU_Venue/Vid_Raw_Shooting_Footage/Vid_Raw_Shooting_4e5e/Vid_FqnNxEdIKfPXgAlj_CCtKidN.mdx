## What This Video Is

A 30 minute 15 second recording filed under the opaque name
`FqnNxEdIKfPXgAlj`, with no poster and no source URL recorded. It is one of the
longest single artefacts in this cluster and one of the most valuable, because
it does not stop when the event does.

Structurally it is two videos joined at one moment. The first stretch is a fixed
wide shot of the "PROVE ME WRONG" tent at Utah Valley University, running the
Q&A through several exchanges. Then the shot is fired, and the recording becomes
a first-person handheld walk-around: the operator moves through the plaza, up
concrete stairs, along the breezeways and through campus buildings,
**interviewing bystanders as the evacuation is happening**. That second half is
what makes this file worth reading closely. It is contemporaneous witness
testimony collected within minutes, before anyone had a narrative.

One warning about the automated description attached to this file: it
characterises the pivotal moment as "a physical altercation" and "a scuffle"
breaking out behind the tent. That is wrong. The transcript is unambiguous that
what occurs is a gunshot and the crowd's reaction to it. Anyone working from the
machine description alone would be badly misled.

## What Is Said

**Before.** The Q&A running when the recording starts is theological - an
extended, good-humoured exchange between Kirk and an LDS questioner about
archaeological evidence, the golden plates, and the resurrection, with Kirk
repeatedly stressing "I love Mormons" and "we just have a different worldview."
It is ordinary campus debate.

The final question is not. A questioner introduces himself, references a
transgender mass shooter some weeks earlier and a proposal to revoke gun rights,
and asks:

> "Do you know how many transgender Americans have been mass shooters over the
> last 10 years?"

Kirk answers, "Too many." The questioner counters, "Okay, now five is a lot,
right?" Kirk replies, "I'm gonna give you some credit," and asks the follow-up:

> "Do you know how many mass shooters there have been in America over the last
> ten years? Counting or not counting gang violence?"

The questioner says "Great." That is the last word before the shot. Several
bystanders in the later interviews return to this on their own, unprompted:
"what a coincidence that he was talking about gun violence and then all of a
sudden this happens", and "the second speaker was the one talking about guns...
and then the next thing you know."

**The moment.** What follows on the audio is immediate and unstructured: "There's
been a shooting. We heard shots. We heard shots. You guys okay?... Hey, be safe,
buddy." A voice adds, "Apparently there's been a shooting. I don't know who's
been shot."

**After.** The operator begins reporting: "We are here live at Utah Valley State
University. There's been a shooting at the Charlie Kirk event, the TPUSA event."
He is openly uncertain at first - "I don't know if that was shots or if that was
fireworks, to be honest."

The wound reports arrive by relay before they arrive by observation. A parent on
the phone to a child who was near the front says, "Charlie Kirk got shot in the
neck is what my kid is saying... This is unconfirmed." Moments later: "We have
multiple eyewitnesses saying that Charlie Kirk has been shot in the neck. They
saw blood spray." Then a security account, second-hand: "I just heard them say
the active shooter. Charlie Kirk has been shot... is what the security detail
just said."

The most substantial interview is with a man who gives his first name as Eric
and places himself "about the third level back, so probably 50 feet from
Charlie":

> "I heard a shot, which took my attention away from Charlie. And when I looked
> back at him after the echoes... there was blood protruding from his neck or
> his head about six inches or more, literally gushing out. Neck or head - I'm
> not quite sure, because his head was tilted back."

Asked whether the shooter was close or distant, he says he does not know, and
his description of the sound is worth quoting exactly because it is a naive
observation made before any theory existed:

> "It sounded like a higher calibre, like a 9 millimetre. It didn't sound like a
> .45 or anything... It didn't sound that deep to me. It almost sounded like a
> cherry bomb."

A second witness, from the third row, describes what he saw rather than heard:
"I heard the shot ring out... I saw him slump over in his chair forward. He had
blood coming from the left side of his neck, down his shirt."

Two further things surface in the aftermath audio and both are threads the
investigation follows elsewhere. First, an early detention: "The police said it
could have come from up on the roof, but they arrested a guy down right in front
of Charlie, so there might have been." A bystander separately reports that a
photographer standing near the camera had photographed the person who was
detained and was taking it to UVU police. Second, a bag search - an official
voice is heard asking someone, "Can we see your backpack?"

A man who had been near the underpass gives a movement account: "maybe 30
seconds before the shot, walking up the stairs all the way to the side of the
bridge underpass area because I was looking for the line to ask him a question.
It came to our attention that the line was on the other side. So I went around
and ended just by the structure thing. And that's when we heard the gunshot."

## What The Footage Shows

* **Opening stretch** - a static, high-angle wide shot of the tent, the speaker
  at the table, the barriers, security standing nearby, and a dense crowd of
  students on the terraced steps, many in red caps. Bright midday sun.
* **The moment** - the frame shakes; the crowd breaks. People go flat or run.
* **Aftermath** - handheld first-person movement: up concrete steps, past
  glass-walled buildings and large green "UVU" signage, through open-air
  corridors and a campus bookstore corridor, stopping repeatedly to interview
  students.

The recording does **not** show the wound, the shooter, or the roof. Its
first-half framing is fixed on the tent from a distance and its second half is
turned outward at witnesses. Everything about the injury in this file is
testimony, not imagery.

Read as evidence, its strengths are unusual for this cluster: it is long,
continuous, unedited, and it captures uncertainty honestly - the operator says
he cannot tell shots from fireworks, the parent labels his own relay
"unconfirmed", and Eric refuses to guess at range. The earliest descriptions
here all converge on a **neck wound with heavy immediate bleeding on the left
side**, from at least three independent people who did not have time to
coordinate. That convergence is the single most useful thing in the file.

Its limits are equally clear. Almost every statement is second-hand or
third-hand within minutes of the event, the acoustic descriptions are lay
impressions from people in a hard-surfaced plaza where echo is severe, and the
"arrested a guy right in front of Charlie" line is a rumour circulating in the
crowd, not a confirmed detention. This page records those statements as what
witnesses said at the time. It does not treat any of them as findings.
