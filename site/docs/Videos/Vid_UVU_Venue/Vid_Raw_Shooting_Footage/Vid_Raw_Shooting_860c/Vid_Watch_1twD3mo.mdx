## What This Video Is

A 59 second handheld capture held in the investigation's archive as `Watch for the.mp4`, carrying the watermark @IN2THINAIR in the lower right. It is filmed from an elevated position looking down and across the Utah Valley University plaza, and it is one of the widest crowd-behaviour records in the corpus.

It is not aimed at the stage. The camera is looking at the crowd, and it keeps looking at the crowd right through the moment the event ends. That makes it the wrong clip for anyone trying to see what happened at the table and the right clip for anyone trying to understand what several hundred people did in the twenty seconds afterward.

## What Is Said

There is no narration. What the microphone picks up is bystander talk, and the speech pass recovers enough of it to be worth quoting:

> "Charlie Kirk brings a pretty good audience."

That is before. Then, after the sound:

> "There was some shots." — "Really?" — "There's just some shooting going on..." — "Somebody had just shot here at Utah Valley State." — "One shot."

Those lines are useful precisely because they are uninformed. Nobody near this microphone knew what had happened; they are working it out aloud in real time. The last of them, "one shot", is a contemporaneous bystander count from someone standing in the plaza, and the number of shots is one of the live disputes in this investigation. It is a single unverified impression from an unidentified person, not a finding — but it is a datum recorded in the first seconds, before anyone had a narrative to fit it to.

## What The Footage Shows

A continuous handheld pan with no cuts. The stored log:

* **00:00 - 00:11** — a high-angle pan left to right across a packed courtyard, concrete walkways, students gathered. Ordinary event crowd. A man with a stroller is in the near foreground for the first few seconds.
* **00:11 - 00:13** — the pop. The camera shakes slightly. The crowd's movement changes from standing to running within about a second.
* **00:13 - 00:38** — the pan tracks the dispersal. People run across grass and walkways in every direction at once.
* **00:38 - 00:41** — the camera swings right onto a concrete ramp where people are rushing up and down the stairs.
* **00:41 - 00:58** — back across the central plaza, now largely emptied, with stragglers still moving away quickly.

The crowd is described as mostly college-aged, in ordinary summer clothing, going from standing and socialising to running in a fraction of a second.

Two things are worth drawing out. The dispersal is omnidirectional — people scatter away from where they are rather than away from a single identified direction, which is what a crowd does when nobody knows where a sound came from. And the reaction is effectively instantaneous, with the movement change visible within the same two-second window as the audio event, which fixes the sound and the panic to each other on one continuous recording with no editing between them.

A note on the frames: young people, including at least one child, run through this footage. They are bystanders and are not identified here.
