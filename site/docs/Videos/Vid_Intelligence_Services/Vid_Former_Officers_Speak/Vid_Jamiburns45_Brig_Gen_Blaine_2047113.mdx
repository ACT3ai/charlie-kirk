## What This Video Is

A six-minute-sixteen broadcast interview segment, circulated on X by [@jamiburns45](/Influencers/x/jamiburns45). Two guests appear with a host: **Rob O'Neill**, the former Navy SEAL Team Six operator publicly known for the bin Laden raid, and **Brigadier General Blaine Holt**, a retired Air Force officer and pilot. The transcript renders the general's surname as "Hall"; it is Holt.

The format is a straightforward television or streaming interview — host asks, guests answer, no B-roll, no graphics. What distinguishes it is the guests' stated qualifications: the host opens by invoking O'Neill's ballistics background directly, and both men repeatedly ground their objections in their own military experience rather than in documents.

Everything below is what these two men said on air. It is their reading of the public record and their opinion of it. None of it is a finding, none of it is corroborated on the clip, and this page does not adopt any of it.

## What Is Said

**O'Neill's framing.** He begins by rejecting the label before it is applied: "it's not like I'm out there saying big lie or conspiracy, or someone did it. I'm just saying, I've been in a lot of shootings... and this thing looked shady off the bat."

**The shirt movement.** His most-quoted line in this clip: "I've killed people closer than I saw the camera, and I've never seen a shirt move like that. That looks like an explosion to me." That is a visual impression from footage, offered by someone claiming relevant experience. It is not a forensic conclusion and he does not present it as one.

**The response on scene.** He says what troubles him is "the fact that no one started doing first aid to Charlie Kirk, they were more concerned about getting SIM cards out of cameras, removing cameras, and then getting him off the X somehow. Didn't see a lot of concern for his life. It seemed like just cleaning it up." He adds, "I've been told they paved it over a couple days later," explicitly flagging that as something he was told.

**Erika Kirk.** He remarks on her public forgiveness of the accused, saying it "technically says that was him, because I forgive him," and contrasts it with the treatment of Candace Owens for "having an opinion or asking questions." That is his commentary on public statements. It is reproduced here as his view and nothing more; this site makes no claim about her conduct or motives.

**The rifle and the spine.** Asked about the account that the round struck the spine, both men reject it flatly. Holt: "No. That's not what happened... I'm with Rob. Something exploded on him. But I don't have to get into the details, because I'm not in a court of law having to prove a darn thing" — a candid statement of the standard he is applying. O'Neill: ".30-06, .30 calibre invented in 1906, to kill big game. If you hit something in the spine, especially a human -- I've shot ballistic gelatin all the time. I've never shot a human with .30-06, but I know it would do a lot more than that." He adds that the wound pattern read to him as "left to right, not from front to back."

**Holt's central objection.** "There are millions of vets with Rob's training... we have enough training to know better than the garbage that was foisted upon us from the Justice Department that day where they seemingly solved the case in about eight hot seconds." He questions the plausibility of the profile: "You're not going to take a .30-06 with a kid who's never killed anybody up onto a roof. He takes his first life and does all the things that Rob says he did." His stated puzzle is not who did it but why the account was presented as it was: "they know that we know better and yet they foist this as the narrative anyway. Why would you do that?"

**The mechanical sequence.** O'Neill runs through the reported sequence as a list of implausibilities: "the screwdriver that probably wouldn't have worked to take it apart or put it together in the first place was left on the rooftop anyway, so he jumped off the roof, and put it back together without the screwdriver... wrapped it up, and put it nicely in a bunch of trees, and then ran off and started texting someone like English was a second language." He ends on the standard he wants applied: "I hope we find out the truth in court."

**George Zinn and Zachariah Qureshi.** The host raises the 71-year-old man who, in the immediate aftermath, said aloud that he had shot Kirk. Holt says of him only that "there's been a lot of reporting that he's got an interesting past all unto himself, and he's been at several scenes... needs to be looked into." He then redirects to what he considers the sharper question — the roughly 90-minute window between a "we got our guy" statement and its retraction: "that's 90 minutes that a real killer could have fled the scene."

**Process, not accusation.** Holt's closing is a procedural request rather than a charge: "follow the normal, boring steps to a murder investigation and just follow them transparently for the country because it's high interest and we'll all be fine and we'll stop asking questions."

**The JFK frame.** Both men reach for 1963. Holt: "I think a lot of people never thought something like that could happen again, because now everybody's got a cell phone, everybody's got a camera, except for this day in September." O'Neill: "We didn't get the truth in 1963... And I feel like it just keeps happening." The segment ends with the host turning to Butler, Pennsylvania, and the clip cuts.

## What The Footage Shows

Nothing beyond an interview. Three people talking, cut between them in the ordinary way, with no supporting material shown at any point — no footage of the shooting, no ballistic imagery, no photograph of the rooftop or the recovered items, no document.

That absence matters more here than in most clips, because much of what is said is a reading of specific visual evidence. When O'Neill says he has "never seen a shirt move like that," the shirt is not on screen. When both men reject the spine account, no wound description is shown. The viewer is asked to accept two experienced men's recollection of material they are describing from memory.

The value of the segment is therefore its testimony, not its demonstration: two people with relevant backgrounds, on the record, under their own names, saying the official account does not match their experience — and saying so with an unusual degree of self-limitation. Holt states outright that he is not in a court and does not have to prove anything, and O'Neill says he hopes the truth comes out in court. Both frames should travel with the quotes.
