## What Is Said

The commentator sets the scene from memory and gets some of it approximately right and some of it loose. His version: a witness contacted the FBI about video he had taken, and "they were like, hey, you know what you should do with that? You should delete it," on the reasoning that "it'll be too emotional for you to watch." He says the matter escalated through several levels of seniority and that someone "ended up calling back multiple days later and was just like, hey, we just want to confirm that you deleted that video." He then adds the detail that gives the clip its force: the witness "not only had those photos and videos backed up onto his cell phone, but onto multiple devices, including a hard drive that wasn't connected to the internet, and uploaded to multiple social media platforms," and that the copies on the phone are "all gone." His own summary of his position is blunt: "I don't know what the hell is going on with this investigation, but all I know is it stinks."

From 1:07 the witness speaks for himself, and his claim is narrower than the framing around it. He asks how files can be "just gone from your phone... erased from your phone" when the same material survives on hard drives that were never connected to the internet. He describes his phone "turning different colors," lagging while he typed, and "watching it type different things that I'm not typing." He acknowledges how this sounds: "I know I sound like a cuckoo when I'm saying this." He says the loss was from his camera library rather than from TikTok, and he says nothing at all in this clip about who he believes caused it. His closing instruction to viewers is preservation, not accusation: "Please, save, save, save... screen record them, spread them."

That distinction matters. The commentator supplies the actor. The witness reports only the effect.
