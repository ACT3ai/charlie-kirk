# Tony Graf

> **Disclaimer:** Inclusion on this page does not imply that this individual had knowledge of, participated in, or committed any illegal or immoral act. This page documents their publicly known connection to the events of September 10, 2025 for investigative reference only.

> **Living Person Notice:** Tony Graf is a living person. We document his public judicial role only, with attribution. We do **not** assert wrongdoing.

## Investigative Value

Judge Tony F. Graf Jr. is the Fourth District Court judge who is publicly reported to preside over the prosecution of the accused shooter in the Charlie Kirk case. According to court reporting summarized in the project's investigation notes, Graf presided over the defendant's initial court appearance (held by video feed, on or about September 16, 2025) and has continued to handle later stages of the case, including motions over the release and sealing of evidence. These are ordinary judicial functions and are described here only to map who occupies which official role in the proceedings.

Graf was appointed to the Fourth District Court by Governor Spencer Cox on May 2, 2025. See the [court](/court/overview) section and the [Tyler Robinson](/Tyler_Robinson/overview) section for the surrounding proceedings.

## What is reported

- Per the project's notes, Graf presided over the accused shooter's first court appearance and ordered the defendant held without bail on charges including aggravated murder.
- According to those same notes, Graf was appointed to the Fourth District Court by Gov. Spencer Cox in May 2025, filling a vacancy created by the retirement of Judge Robert Lunnen (effective August 1, 2025).
- Reporting summarized in the file notes that a coalition of news organizations has sought to unseal certain evidence, and that related rulings fall to the presiding judge.
- **Counterpoint:** Presiding over a high-profile case, and ruling on sealing or evidence motions, are routine judicial duties. Nothing in the source material shows that Judge Graf did anything improper; being newly appointed before a case is not evidence of wrongdoing.
