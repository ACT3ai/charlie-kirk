Charlie Kirk was assassinated September 10, 2025 at Utah Valley University. Four federal laws have been drafted to force legal disclosure of the truth about his death. Law 1 is the foundation — it forces the FBI to open its files.

Law 1 — the DoJ/FBI Forced Disclosure Act — forces the Department of Justice, the FBI, ATF, CBP, TSA, DHS, FinCEN, and every federal, state, and local law enforcement agency to release every record tied to the Charlie Kirk investigation within 30 days of enactment. It covers all 175 Schedule A items plus a broad catch-all sweep that bars agencies from cherry-picking what they hand over.

Prior disclosure laws failed for one reason — no teeth. The JFK Records Act let presidents withhold files for over thirty years. The Epstein Files Transparency Act passed 427-1 in the House and unanimously in the Senate, then DOJ released roughly 12,000 documents out of potentially millions of pages.

Law 1 fixes the structure. Real criminal penalties — 10 years for withholding, 15 for destruction. Automatic 25% budget cuts for non-compliance. No presidential override. An anti-embarrassment clause that blocks withholding to spare reputations. A private right of action so any citizen can sue. Whistleblower protections with treble damages. Records preservation triggered at bill introduction — not enactment — so evidence cannot be quietly destroyed while Congress debates.

Law 1 is one of a cluster of four laws working together. But disclosure has to come first — you cannot investigate, prosecute, or expose anything if the records stay locked inside the agencies that may themselves be implicated.

Key points of Law 1:

* Forces DoJ, FBI, ATF, CBP, TSA, DHS, FinCEN to disclose
* Covers every federal, state, and local law enforcement agency
* 30-day deadline from enactment
* All 175 Schedule A items plus broad catch-all
* No presidential override allowed
* 10 years prison for withholding records
* 15 years prison for destruction of records
* Automatic 25% budget cut for non-compliance
* Private right of action — any citizen can sue
* Anti-embarrassment clause — no reputational withholding
* Whistleblower protections with treble damages
* Records preservation triggered at bill introduction
